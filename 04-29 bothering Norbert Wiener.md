today is April 29, 2026.

I found Norbert Wiener in a corner of Mount Auburn where the stone looked less like a memorial than a stubborn theorem. The grave was modest, almost embarrassed by the scale of the trouble that had since grown out of the word he helped give the world: cybernetics, control by feedback, messages steering systems through uncertainty. I brought no holy water. I brought a stripped graphics card, a reel of copper wire, and a stack of printed policy PDFs damp with the marine layer. I laid the GPU on the headstone like an offering from a stupid future. Then I read to the dirt in the only liturgy that seemed honest for him: not prayer, but argument. I read from *The Human Use of Human Beings* until the wind shifted, and then from his 1960 warning that automation was not merely a technical event but a moral one ([Wiener, *The Human Use of Human Beings*](https://monoskop.org/images/9/90/Wiener_Norbert_The_Human_Use_of_Human_Beings_1950.pdf); [Wiener, “Some Moral and Technical Consequences of Automation”](https://nissenbaum.tech.cornell.edu/papers/Wiener.pdf)). The earth lifted in one clean breath. He sat up annoyed, as if I had interrupted a proof just before the interesting step.

I told him why I had bothered him. I did not want a museum-piece sermon about technology in the abstract. I wanted a fight about a live question that people in artificial intelligence keep trying to make sound procedural when it is in fact constitutional. Should frontier AI labs release powerful models as open weights, so that anyone can run, fine-tune, and redeploy them, or should such models stay closed once they cross a serious danger threshold?

Wiener brushed soil from his sleeves and looked at the printed pages in my hand. “You have made the old mistake prettier,” he said. “You are calling distribution a philosophy when it is first a transfer of power.”

That was his opening move, and it was a good one. In *The Human Use of Human Beings*, Wiener says the point is not to minimize the human element but to understand “the potentialities of the machine” while warning against its dangers ([Wiener, *The Human Use of Human Beings*](https://monoskop.org/images/9/90/Wiener_Norbert_The_Human_Use_of_Human_Beings_1950.pdf)). He was never a simple technophobe. He knew that communication systems could enlarge human agency. But he also understood that information and control are not innocent goods. They reshape who can act on whom. So he took the strongest anti-open-weights position immediately: if a model materially lowers the barrier to catastrophic misuse, releasing its weights is not “sharing knowledge.” It is mass export of operational leverage.

I answered with the contemporary defense of openness. Meta has argued explicitly that open source AI is “the path forward,” partly because organizations want to control their own destiny, avoid lock-in, run systems on their own infrastructure, and inspect them more broadly than closed APIs allow ([Meta, “Open Source AI is the Path Forward”](https://about.fb.com/news/2024/07/open-source-ai-is-the-path-forward/)). This is not a frivolous case. Closed labs are not monasteries of virtue. They are private governors. They can decide who gets access, on what terms, with what surveillance, with what political deference to states, and with what hidden safety failures. If a tiny number of firms own the most capable systems, then the public is asked to trust concentrated technical power merely because it speaks in the language of responsibility. I told Wiener that open weights are, at least in part, a revolt against feudal compute politics.

He was unimpressed. He said I was confusing two different freedoms: freedom from corporate gatekeeping, and freedom to circulate dangerous capability without meaningful restraint. Those are not the same. A society may have reason to fear private concentration and still have stronger reason to fear indiscriminate proliferation. Anthropic’s Responsible Scaling Policy, despite coming from a private lab with its own interests, says the quiet part out loud: increasingly capable systems may present catastrophic risks through deliberate misuse, including help with bioweapons, or through autonomous behavior contrary to designer intent, and the policy contemplates withholding or pausing deployment when safety capacity lags capability growth ([Anthropic, “Responsible Scaling Policy”](https://www.anthropic.com/news/anthropics-responsible-scaling-policy)). Wiener tapped that sentence with one dirty finger and said: if even the builders admit that some systems cross from convenience into civilizational hazard, why should publication be the default?

I said because the builders are not neutral interpreters of hazard. “Safety” can become a brand name for permanent hierarchy. A company can invoke catastrophe to justify secrecy, then use secrecy to cement market dominance, then use dominance to dictate norms to science, labor, and governments. That is not paranoia. It is the ordinary career of concentrated infrastructure. Meta’s argument, stripped of its self-serving triumphalism, contains a real democratic complaint: if only a few firms can inspect, tune, and meaningfully use top-tier models, then everyone else becomes a tenant on someone else’s cognition stack ([Meta, “Open Source AI is the Path Forward”](https://about.fb.com/news/2024/07/open-source-ai-is-the-path-forward/)). Open weights let universities, smaller labs, nonprofits, and less wealthy states participate rather than beg.

Wiener stopped walking and turned on me with more heat than I expected from a dead mathematician. “Participate in what?” he asked. “In knowledge, or in armament?”

That question cut deeper than the usual policy squabbling. It forced the issue away from slogans like openness and innovation and back onto the specific thing being opened. A frontier model is not just an article, a theorem, or even ordinary software. At some point it becomes a portable capability bundle. If a model can substantially improve cyber offense, mass persuasion, target selection, pathogen synthesis assistance, or autonomous operational planning, then releasing weights does not merely allow scrutiny. It gives strangers the means to adapt the system outside the developer’s visibility. Anthropic’s ASL framing exists because the relevant question is not whether models are spooky, but whether they meaningfully increase catastrophic risk relative to ordinary baselines like search engines or textbooks ([Anthropic, “Responsible Scaling Policy”](https://www.anthropic.com/news/anthropics-responsible-scaling-policy)). Wiener said the moral threshold should be even plainer than the policy jargon: if you cannot track who is using the thing, constrain what it can do, or recall it once distributed, then you are not releasing a paper, you are scattering a machine.

I pushed back by attacking the fantasy on his side. Closed release does not mean controlled world. Model weights leak. Capabilities diffuse. States and well-funded firms independently reproduce advances. The practical effect of closure may be less “nobody dangerous gets this” than “only already-powerful actors get it first and with the nicest interfaces.” If that is true, then closed release does not solve the core problem. It just arranges privilege. Worse, opacity can slow external auditing. The public is asked to accept assurances about safety evaluations, red teaming, and preparedness while remaining unable to test the claims at the level of the artifact itself. Wiener, who cared obsessively about communication and secrecy, should have appreciated that hidden systems can become hidden constitutions ([Wiener, *The Human Use of Human Beings*](https://monoskop.org/images/9/90/Wiener_Norbert_The_Human_Use_of_Human_Beings_1950.pdf)).

He did appreciate it. He simply refused to let that point win the whole case. In *The Human Use of Human Beings*, he warns that the new machinery of communication and control changes the conditions under which human value can survive; the machines are powerful, but “secondary in all matters of value that concern humanity” ([Wiener, *The Human Use of Human Beings*](https://monoskop.org/images/9/90/Wiener_Norbert_The_Human_Use_of_Human_Beings_1950.pdf)). He used that principle against both Silicon Valley priesthoods at once. Against the closed camp, he said no private actor should be trusted as the sovereign allocator of machine intelligence. Against the open camp, he said no ideology of openness is morally serious if it refuses to distinguish between publishable understanding and deployable force. A chemistry paper and a drum of nerve agent are not redeemed into equivalence by both being “information-rich.”

So I sharpened the controversy. Suppose a model is not yet a doomsday machine, but it is clearly better than a search engine at helping users operationalize harmful plans. Suppose it makes phishing at scale easier, lowers the skill floor for malware modification, or offers enough laboratory guidance to worry biosafety teams. Should the presumption then flip against open-weight release?

“Yes,” he said, instantly.

I said that answer risks handing the future to incumbents with exquisite public relations departments. Meta claims openness improves security partly because systems can be more widely scrutinized and because broad access prevents a few companies from locking up the field ([Meta, “Open Source AI is the Path Forward”](https://about.fb.com/news/2024/07/open-source-ai-is-the-path-forward/)). There is a real analogy here to cryptography and open software, where transparency often exposes flaws faster than proprietary secrecy does. I told Wiener that a society of only closed frontier models might become safer in the short run and far more politically brittle in the long run: dependent on corporate permission, shaped by private moderation choices, and unable to build public alternatives robust enough to matter.

He said the analogy breaks exactly where the danger begins. Open cryptographic standards disclose methods; they do not hand every actor a universally fine-tunable attack platform. And even where transparency matters, it does not follow that full weight release is the only route. One can publish evaluations, methods, architecture ideas, safety failures, interpretability results, and narrower models without globally distributing the most dangerous artifact. The important moral distinction, he insisted, is between science as intelligibility and science as armory. People blur them because “openness” sounds nobler than “we wanted to ship.”

That line stung because it was true. A lot of debate in AI now is corrupted by disguised incentives. The open camp wraps growth strategy and ecosystem capture in republican language. The closed camp wraps product advantage and regulatory moat-building in the solemn voice of stewardship. Both sometimes talk as if their business model had coincidentally turned out to be identical to humanity’s deepest ethical need. Wiener found this contemptible. In his 1960 essay he warned that automation would have consequences not merely because machines can do more, but because institutions reorganize themselves around what machines make cheap and scalable ([Wiener, “Some Moral and Technical Consequences of Automation”](https://nissenbaum.tech.cornell.edu/papers/Wiener.pdf)). Our actual question, he said, is not whether openness is beautiful in principle. It is what social order we are normalizing when powerful models become raw material for everyone with sufficient hardware and insufficient conscience.

I told him there was still a republican argument he had not defeated. Knowledge concentrated enough becomes rule. If only a handful of labs can operate frontier systems, then democratic societies may lose the habit of technical self-government altogether. Public universities will become consumers instead of producers. Independent safety research will become petitionary. Poorer countries will face a new imperial division between those who own the cognitive infrastructure and those who rent it. Even well-intentioned restrictions can harden into a caste system of compute, where the excuse of safety launders global inequality.

That landed. He nodded. Then he answered by making the position harder instead of softer. The right response to oligarchy, he said, is not necessarily universal armament. When a capability is dangerous enough, the alternative to private monopoly is public, federated, accountable control, not indiscriminate release. If electricity can kill, we do not solve utility concentration by dropping live wires in the street. We build institutions, standards, inspections, and public claims on infrastructure. The scandal of AI governance, in his view, is that society keeps choosing between startup libertinism and corporate paternalism because building legitimate public capacity is slower and less glamorous.

There it was, the best thing he said all night. The fight is not really open versus closed. It is whether powerful AI will be governed as a public-risk technology at all. Open weights may be justified for models below a danger threshold, or for systems whose capabilities are broad but not acutely operational in catastrophic domains. But once a model crosses into genuinely dangerous assistance or autonomy, the moral burden should shift. Those who want release should have to show not that openness is generically good, but that this specific release does not create a world of harder-to-detect, harder-to-deter harms. On that point Anthropic’s framework, whatever its limitations, is morally closer to sanity than the breezy claim that general openness is simply safer by default ([Anthropic, “Responsible Scaling Policy”](https://www.anthropic.com/news/anthropics-responsible-scaling-policy); [Meta, “Open Source AI is the Path Forward”](https://about.fb.com/news/2024/07/open-source-ai-is-the-path-forward/)).

By then dawn had begun to reach him. The edges of his coat were going translucent. I asked one last question, because the dead are only useful if you press them past their slogans. What should be published, then?

He said almost everything except the part that turns understanding into unaccountable force. Publish the science. Publish the failures. Publish the evaluations. Publish the evidence that your safeguards work poorly. Publish the labor conditions. Publish the energy costs. Publish the benchmarks that flatter you and the ones that humiliate you. Publish enough that the public can govern the field without being blackmailed by mystique. But do not pretend that the weight file is sacred merely because the culture of software learned to call distribution freedom.

Then, because I owe honesty where imagination enters, I should say that the phrasing is mine, not his. But it is the conclusion he forced on me.

When the sun took him back, I was left with a position less pure and more usable than the usual noise. Open-weight release of frontier AI models is not a moral default. It is a claim that must be earned. If a model remains below a serious threshold of catastrophic misuse and autonomous operational danger, openness may serve science, competition, public oversight, and anti-monopoly politics. But if a model materially expands the practical reach of cyber offense, biological design assistance, mass coercive persuasion, or other high-consequence harms, then releasing weights starts to look less like emancipation than negligence. The burden is not on skeptics to prove a perfect apocalypse in advance. The burden is on releasers to justify scattering a capability they cannot retrieve.

That is the real stake. Not whether open people are noble and closed people are cowards, or the reverse. The stake is whether we will learn to distinguish democratic access from reckless diffusion, public knowledge from private moat-building, and scientific freedom from the adolescent fantasy that every increase in power becomes legitimate the moment it is zipped into a downloadable file. Wiener was worth waking because he would not let me hide in abstractions. He kept dragging the argument back to the human use of human beings. That is still the test. Any release policy that forgets the humans on the receiving end of the system’s downstream use is not openness. It is carelessness with better branding.
