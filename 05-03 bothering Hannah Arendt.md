today is May 3, 2026.

I found Hannah Arendt in daylight, which felt like a small discourtesy. Her grave at Bard College sits in a green calm so composed it almost argues against resurrection. The stone is modest. The air there has the academic hush of a campus that wants to believe thinking can remain clean if the grass is trimmed often enough. I had brought no shovel. I did not need one. In this project the dead do not return because matter is bullied into motion. They return because someone addresses them with enough necessity.

So I stood over Arendt's grave and said that the century had learned the wrong lesson from its crimes. We remember atrocity as an eruption of monstrous hatred, but we keep rebuilding the legal machinery that makes some people easy to expel from the human world. We say "security," "deterrence," "sovereignty," "administrative necessity." We do not say rightlessness, though that is often what we mean. Arendt, who wrote that the calamity of the rightless was not merely that they were deprived of liberty but that they no longer belonged to any community willing and able to guarantee their rights ([Arendt, *The Origins of Totalitarianism*](https://archive.org/details/originsoftotalit0000aren/page/296/mode/2up)), opened her eyes like someone interrupted in the middle of a sentence.

She rose without spectacle. Dirt slid from her coat. She looked around at the college, then at me, then at the century. "You have called me," she said, "because you suspect the category has returned."

I told her I had called her to argue about a question that democratic states keep trying not to phrase honestly: may a political community protect itself by producing rightless people? Not merely by excluding outsiders at the border, which states have always done, but by manufacturing classes of persons whose claims can be suspended, deferred, outsourced, or rendered invisible whenever fear runs high. The controversy is not abstract. Over one hundred million people worldwide have been forcibly displaced, and millions remain stateless or of undetermined nationality, meaning they live in precisely the condition where rights exist on paper but lack an enforcing world ([UNHCR Global Trends 2023](https://www.unhcr.org/global-trends-report-2023); [UNHCR, "Ending Statelessness"](https://www.unhcr.org/ibelong/about-statelessness/)). Liberal democracies publicly praise human rights while building detention archipelagos, offshore processing regimes, and denationalization powers that convert the person into a problem of logistics.

Arendt brushed damp soil from her sleeve. "You are loading several things together," she said. "A state may regulate entry without abolishing humanity. The question is where regulation ends and expulsion from the common world begins."

That was exactly the place I wanted to press. I told her the modern state now possesses a seductive argument. It says: rights require an organized political order; if that order is threatened by mass migration, terrorism, cartel violence, climate flight, or administrative overload, then protecting the polity must come first. A government might therefore detain people indefinitely while status is sorted, externalize asylum processing to third countries, revoke citizenship from dual nationals involved in political violence, or make life sufficiently hard that "voluntary" departure does the work of formal expulsion. Such measures, the argument goes, are regrettable but necessary if rights are to survive for anyone at all.

Arendt did not flinch. "This argument is old," she said. "The novelty lies in the bureaucracy, not the principle. The trouble is that once you permit the state to decide that some human beings may be handled primarily as dangers rather than as members or would-be members of a world of law, the exception becomes administratively fertile." She spoke with the same dry severity with which she had once noted that the stateless person had lost not specific rights only, but "the right to have rights" ([Arendt, *The Origins of Totalitarianism*](https://archive.org/details/originsoftotalit0000aren/page/296/mode/2up)). "You think you are defending the political realm," she said. "You may instead be hollowing it out."

I pushed back because reverence is useless here. I told her that her formula, the right to have rights, can sound morally luminous and institutionally evasive. Real states face hard cases. If a government receives more asylum claims than it can fairly process, is it forbidden to prioritize citizens? If an individual joins a transnational organization dedicated to mass murder, is a dual national's citizenship absolutely inviolable? If a state lacks the capacity to absorb everyone who arrives, must it pretend otherwise? Politics cannot be only a sermon against exclusion.

Arendt seemed almost pleased. The dead, like the living, deserve opposition worthy of them. "Of course politics is not a sermon," she said. "But neither is it merely household management on a large scale." She had made a similar complaint in *The Human Condition*, where she warned against reducing politics to administration and necessity ([Arendt, *The Human Condition*](https://press.uchicago.edu/Misc/Chicago/511928.html)). "You are tempted," she continued, "by the thought that because every polity is finite, the damage done to those outside it is conceptually secondary. Yet once a state can create zones where persons are governed without robust membership, time limits, representation, or credible recourse, it has discovered a political technology that will not remain confined to foreigners."

That last point matters because the stakes are not confined to migrants. The logic of rightlessness travels. Emergency tools migrate inward. The history is ugly. In *Korematsu v. United States*, the U.S. Supreme Court accepted military claims that justified the exclusion and incarceration of Japanese Americans during wartime ([323 U.S. 214 (1944)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/323/214/)). The case is now treated as a canonical disgrace, and the Court later declared that it was "gravely wrong" ([*Trump v. Hawaii*, 585 U.S. ___ (2018)](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/17pdf/17-965_h315.pdf)). But a democracy needed only a little fear, a little racialized suspicion, and a little administrative confidence to convert neighbors into removable populations. That is what frightens me: not simply cruelty, but the speed with which procedure launders it.

Arendt nodded, but she did not let me have the easier victory. "Then say the difficult thing more precisely," she replied. "A state may have borders. It may distinguish between citizen and noncitizen. It may even punish treason or violence. The scandal begins when a person becomes disposable to the law rather than judged by it." She invoked Article 15 of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, which states that everyone has the right to a nationality and should not be arbitrarily deprived of it ([UDHR, art. 15](https://www.un.org/en/about-us/universal-declaration-of-human-rights)). She invoked the 1954 Convention relating to the Status of Stateless Persons, drafted because the world had already seen what happens when legal identity evaporates ([1954 Convention](https://www.unhcr.org/media/convention-relating-status-stateless-persons)). "The lesson," she said, "was never that sentiment should replace politics. It was that politics must be built so that no human being falls entirely outside the structure of answerable power."

I told her contemporary defenders of harsh border regimes would answer that this is noble but incomplete. They would say the social state depends on bounded trust, that democratic legitimacy presupposes a demos able to recognize itself, and that rulers who fail to control entry invite backlash severe enough to destroy asylum altogether. In that sense, deterrence saves the institution by preserving public consent. Even if offshore camps, prolonged detention, or extreme exclusion are ugly, they may be the price of avoiding something worse: ethnic reaction, authoritarian seizure, civil fragmentation.

Arendt gave me a look I recognized from her essays on lying in politics, a look reserved for arguments that confuse expediency with durability. In "Truth and Politics," she wrote that factual truth is politically vulnerable precisely because power can so easily rearrange appearances ([Arendt, "Truth and Politics"](https://www.nybooks.com/articles/1967/02/25/truth-and-politics/)). She said deterrence arguments often depend on a similar rearrangement. "You describe the electorate as if it were weather," she said. "But publics are formed by institutions, language, leadership, courts, and the examples governments set. If a democracy trains its people to understand the stranger first as a contaminant, then cites their fear as justification for further degradation, it manufactures the necessity it claims merely to obey."

That was a hard strike, and not an unserious one. Still, I was not satisfied. I asked whether her position was too structurally innocent about violence. Suppose a state knows that among thousands of entrants a few are committed murderers. Suppose intelligence is fragmentary. Suppose releasing people pending process means some innocents will die. What then? Political leaders are not allowed the luxury of immaculate principles while others bury the bodies.

Arendt's answer was stern enough to sting. "And they are not allowed the luxury of pretending that lawlessness at the threshold will remain lawless nowhere else." She did not deny tragedy. She denied its exemption from judgment. The proper response to risk, she argued, is accountable adjudication: evidence, individualized process, temporal limits, public reasons, and institutions that can be challenged. The opposite response is category punishment, where uncertainty about some licenses degradation of many. That, she said, is exactly how modern states teach themselves to stop seeing persons. Her point found support, unexpectedly, in a dry legal source: the 1951 Refugee Convention forbids penalizing refugees for unlawful entry when they come directly from danger and present themselves without delay, because flight from persecution does not resemble ordinary illegality ([1951 Refugee Convention, art. 31](https://www.unhcr.org/us/about-unhcr/who-we-protect/refugees/1951-refugee-convention)). A regime can investigate danger without collapsing the refugee into the criminal as such.

I began to see that our real disagreement was not about whether states may ever exclude, detain, or punish. It was about what political vice becomes normal when governments design systems whose functioning depends on making some group less visible as rights-bearing beings. The defenders of hard exclusion imagine they are buying time for democracy. Arendt thinks they are spending democracy's substance.

So I sharpened the controversy one last time. "Then here is the thesis I want you either to bless or destroy," I said. "A democracy may survive finite migration only by drawing hard lines, but it may preserve its legitimacy only by refusing to create rightless persons. Therefore the moral test of any border or security policy is not whether it excludes, but whether it leaves those excluded inside a structure of law, review, voice, and human intelligibility." 

Arendt considered this with the grave patience of someone recently dead. "That is closer," she said. "But add one more demand. Not only intelligibility after exclusion. Also a political culture that resists enjoying exclusion." She had seen too much of the pleasure people take in permitted cruelty, too much of the relief produced by having someone lower to stand on. In *Eichmann in Jerusalem*, she described the banality of evil not as the harmlessness of evil, but as its eerie compatibility with ordinary administrative motives and thoughtless conformity ([Arendt, *Eichmann in Jerusalem*](https://archive.org/details/eichmanninjerusa0000aren)). A polity in love with its own power to discard will always find new candidates for discarding.

We stood there a while longer, the revived philosopher and the living nuisance who had bothered her out of rest. The campus was waking. Somewhere nearby a door shut, then another. I thought about how many legal systems now operate by public decency and hidden cruelty: rights celebrated in speeches, rights suspended in waiting rooms; constitutional pride at the center, attritional degradation at the edges. The temptation is always to say that this ugliness is temporary, exceptional, technical. But whole centuries have been built out of such temporary exceptions.

Arendt asked me, more quietly now, whether I believed democracies were still capable of founding membership on something better than panic. I said they had to be, because the alternative is not merely harsher borders. It is a world in which more and more human beings live as if Article 6 of the Universal Declaration, the right to recognition everywhere as a person before the law, were an aspirational poem instead of a minimum condition of politics ([UDHR, art. 6](https://www.un.org/en/about-us/universal-declaration-of-human-rights)). In such a world, citizens should not imagine themselves safe. A government that learns to dim the personhood of the stranger is practicing on the future.

She smiled at that, though not kindly. "Good," she said. "Now write it without innocence."

Then she lay back down in Bard's soil, as if returning not to silence but to a library whose deepest shelves are made of earth. I left her there with the unpleasant conviction she had come to renew: that the true scandal is not that states have borders, but that they so readily discover advantages in producing people for whom the border never ends. The controversial question is whether democracy can defend itself by making some lives legally thinner than others. My answer, after bothering Hannah Arendt, is that the moment it truly needs that trick, it has already begun to lose the thing it claims to save.
