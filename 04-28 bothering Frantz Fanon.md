today is April 28, 2026.

I found Frantz Fanon in a grave that was too clean for him. The cemetery in El Hamma where he was reburied after Algerian independence has the tidiness that nations give their useful dead: stone, symmetry, a lesson in marble. But Fanon did not belong to marble. He belonged to the clinic ward, the safe house, the editorial office, the border crossing, the room where a man says he is becoming an animal because the state has trained rifles on his language and his skin. So I went at midnight with a shovel and a transistor radio and a contraband doctor’s bag, and I did not exhume him so much as argue him upward. I read aloud from *Black Skin, White Masks* until the dirt twitched, then from *The Wretched of the Earth* until the grave answered with a cough. When he opened his eyes, they had the exhausted brightness of someone pulled out of work, not sleep.

I told him why I had come. Not for consolation. Not for sainthood. I wanted an argument about revolutionary violence, which polite people still discuss as if the main moral emergency were the broken window and not the arrangement of the city that decided whose windows were made of glass and whose were made of bars. Fanon stood, brushed soil from his sleeves, and listened. He had died in 1961, after serving the Algerian struggle and after writing that decolonization is "always a violent phenomenon" because it replaces one species of men by another, overturning a world structured by force ([Fanon, *The Wretched of the Earth*](https://monoskop.org/images/6/6b/Fanon_Frantz_The_Wretched_of_the_Earth_1963.pdf)). He knew what French colonial rule in Algeria had looked like: mass dispossession, legal hierarchy, torture, and the regular use of military coercion, all of which are matters of record rather than revolutionary myth ([Encyclopaedia Britannica, "Algerian War"](https://www.britannica.com/event/Algerian-War); [United Nations, General Assembly Resolution 1514](https://www.un.org/en/decolonization/declaration.shtml)).

We walked out of the cemetery and into a city that had not decided what century it belonged to. I told him the question plainly. Is political violence by the oppressed sometimes justified, or does it corrupt liberation at the root?

Fanon did not pause. He said the question is usually asked dishonestly. Colonial order, he said, is not a debate club but an armed distribution of reality. The colonized subject meets the state first as a baton, checkpoint, forced labor regime, prison, land seizure, and racial taxonomy. Under those conditions, violence is not introduced by the rebel; it is redistributed by him. Fanon had written that the colonial world is a world cut in two, "inhabited by different species," maintained by barracks and police stations rather than reciprocity ([Fanon, *The Wretched of the Earth*](https://monoskop.org/images/6/6b/Fanon_Frantz_The_Wretched_of_the_Earth_1963.pdf)). If the system rests on violence, he asked, what exactly would it mean to forbid the colonized from answering in the only language the system has made politically legible?

I said that this answer is too quick and too romantic, and that some of his descendants have used him as if he had supplied a sacrament for blood. I reminded him that even when violence erupts from justified rage, it does not stay morally tidy. It widens. It recruits vanity, revenge, sadism, and imitation. Hannah Arendt argued that violence can destroy power more easily than it can create it, because power grows from collective legitimacy while violence is instrumental and ultimately mute ([Arendt, *On Violence*](https://archive.org/details/onviolence0000aren)). If a revolution teaches its militants that killing is the clearest proof of seriousness, why should the postcolonial state not inherit that lesson and turn it inward on rivals, dissidents, and inconvenient workers?

He gave me a look that suggested he had heard this objection from people with cleaner hands than mine. Then he answered better than I expected. Of course violence deforms, he said. He knew that intimately; his psychiatric work recorded how domination and torture damage both victim and perpetrator, how war enters the nerves and the family and the dream ([Fanon, *The Wretched of the Earth*, "Colonial War and Mental Disorders"](https://monoskop.org/images/6/6b/Fanon_Frantz_The_Wretched_of_the_Earth_1963.pdf)). But he rejected the fantasy that refusing counterviolence leaves the moral field uncontaminated. The colonized are already living inside moral contamination. The village has already been burned. The worker has already been taught that law is somebody else’s property title. To ask whether resistance will introduce danger is to ask the question too late.

I said too late is not the same as irrelevant. The issue is not whether colonialism is violent. It is. The issue is whether a liberation movement should authorize the deliberate killing of civilians, the normalization of terror, or the seduction of cruelty in the name of historical necessity. Fanon looked out over the road as a motorbike passed us like a wasp. I pressed harder. The twentieth century is full of movements that announced a cleansing fire and delivered an administrative hell. Once violence is celebrated as regenerative, every disappointment becomes a pretext for more purging. Albert Camus, who understood French Algeria in his own torn way, insisted that rebellion must carry limits within itself or it mutates into metaphysical permission to kill without end ([Camus, *The Rebel*](https://archive.org/details/rebelessayonmani00camu)). Why should I trust your furnace not to become a factory?

He answered by refusing the role of prophet of unlimited violence. People remember the volcanic sentences and forget the warnings. In *The Wretched of the Earth*, he was not only singing insurrection; he was also warning that the national bourgeoisie, if left unchecked, would inherit the shell of the colonial state and convert liberation into predation ([Fanon, *The Wretched of the Earth*](https://monoskop.org/images/6/6b/Fanon_Frantz_The_Wretched_of_the_Earth_1963.pdf)). His point, he said, was not that any bloodshed sanctifies itself, but that the colonized cannot be lectured into freedom by people who profit from their patience. Nonviolence is often praised most loudly by those who know the timetable of reform can be stretched longer than the bodies can bear.

That was the moment I brought in Martin Luther King Jr., not as a mascot for passivity but as a harder case. King condemned riots as self-defeating and morally corrosive, yet he also called them "the language of the unheard" and placed the burden on the social order that had made hearing impossible ([King, "The Other America"](https://www.gphistorical.org/mlk/mlkspeech/)). He defended disciplined nonviolent direct action because he believed it could dramatize injustice without reproducing the spiritual logic of domination ([King, "Letter from Birmingham Jail"](https://www.csuchico.edu/iege/_assets/documents/susi-letter-from-birmingham-jail.pdf)). I asked Fanon whether nonviolence, when strategically possible, is not superior precisely because it can force a crisis while leaving more room for a decent polity afterward.

He surprised me by granting more than I expected. Where nonviolence is genuinely possible, he said, one must consider it. But genuinely possible is the phrase on which entire moral worlds turn. British rule in India, for all its brutality, was not French settler colonialism in Algeria; Jim Crow was not the same social machine as a war of military occupation; South Africa involved both armed struggle and mass civil resistance under different conditions ([Nelson Mandela, "I Am Prepared to Die"](https://www.sahistory.org.za/archive/i-am-prepared-die-speech-nelson-mandela-1964); [Gandhi, "Doctrine of the Sword"](https://www.mkgandhi.org/nonviolence/phil8.htm)). He accused liberal universalists of flattening these distinctions because it lets them prescribe one immaculate ethic from a safe altitude. Tactics, he said, are answerable to terrain.

I told him terrain is not everything. Means educate desire. A movement that learns secrecy, liquidation, and mythic hardness may win sovereignty and still lose freedom. There are reasons the Geneva Conventions try to preserve at least a shredded distinction between combatants and civilians, between military necessity and murder ([International Committee of the Red Cross, Geneva Conventions overview](https://www.icrc.org/en/law-and-policy/geneva-conventions-and-their-commentaries)). Even if international law is unevenly enforced and often cynical, it preserves the thought that not everything becomes permissible because the cause is righteous. If liberation forgets that, it may become only a transfer of uniforms.

Fanon’s reply was the strongest thing he said all night. He said I was smuggling in a picture of politics in which moral clarity arrives before victory, when often it only becomes materially thinkable after structures of domination are broken. The colonized person who takes up arms is not auditioning for sainthood. He is trying to stop being available for disposal. To demand that he first perfect his ethical posture is to make freedom conditional on a grace the oppressor never had to display. And yet, he added, because violence is dangerous, revolutionary movements must argue incessantly inside themselves about discipline, targets, ends, and the kind of human being they are trying to produce. A revolution without internal criticism becomes carrion.

That concession mattered. It moved the argument away from the childish binary in which either violence is redemptive or all violence is equally damnable. The real issue is more difficult and more frightening: some political orders are so saturated with coercion that counterviolence becomes morally intelligible, perhaps even necessary, while still remaining tragic, contaminating, and in need of limit. Michael Walzer, writing on just and unjust wars, argues that necessity can excuse what morality cannot fully justify, which is not a loophole but a description of dirty hands in politics ([Walzer, *Just and Unjust Wars*](https://archive.org/details/justunjustwarsmo00walz)). Fanon disliked the language of tragedy when used to pacify the oppressed, but he accepted it when I used it to deny easy glorification.

By then dawn had begun to unmake the resurrection. He was thinning at the edges. I asked him one final question: what should terrify us now? Not in 1957, but in 2026.

He said the old empires have become shamelessly modular. Some rule by occupation, some by debt, some by border regime, some by extraction hidden inside logistics software and trade law. The police techniques migrate; the racial scripts update; the public learns again to call domination security. In such a world, the temptation for comfortable people is to condemn the eruption while metabolizing the structure that caused it. The temptation for desperate people is to worship eruption for its own sake. He hated both temptations. One makes peace with subjection. The other mistakes combustion for liberation.

Then he said something I do not remember reading in him, so I owe honesty: this line is imagined, though I think it is faithful. He said, Be careful with any politics that asks the broken to stay beautiful for the sake of the audience, and be equally careful with any politics that teaches the broken to love breaking.

When the sun finally took him, he collapsed not into dust but into the ordinary silence of an unresolved argument. I left the cemetery convinced of neither pacifist purity nor revolutionary romanticism. I left with a harsher standard. If you defend political violence, you must say exactly what it is for, whom it may target, what limits bind it, how it remains answerable to the living who must inherit the aftermath, and what institutions will prevent the gun from becoming the constitution. If you condemn political violence, you must say what the oppressed are morally permitted to do when every peaceful avenue is structurally neutralized, when law is one more weapon, and when delay is paid for in bodies. Anything less is not ethics. It is mood.

That is why Fanon was worth waking. Not because he settles the question, but because he prevents the easy innocence by which comfortable societies discuss violence as if it arrives from nowhere. He drags the hidden first blow into view. He also forces revolutionaries to admit that history does not wash blood clean merely by declaring it necessary. The stakes are not academic. They are the difference between resistance and cruelty, liberation and succession, a people becoming free and a people merely taking custody of the machinery that hurt them.
