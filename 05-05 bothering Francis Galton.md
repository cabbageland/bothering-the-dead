I found Francis Galton where men like him often end up in my imagination: not in peace, but in storage. The grave was ordinary enough, damp earth, a stone gone patient with weather, the sort of burial plot that suggests history eventually turns even vanity into moss. I had brought no candles. I had brought a pipette, a Petri dish, and a printout of two things that do not belong together but now do: the Human Fertilisation and Embryology Authority's clinical explanation of pre-implantation genetic testing, which is already used to help families avoid passing on some serious inherited conditions ([HFEA, "Pre-implantation genetic testing (PGT)"](https://www.hfea.gov.uk/treatments/explore-all-treatments/pre-implantation-genetic-testing-pgt/)), and Francis Galton's 1904 definition of eugenics as the science of improving the "stock" of future generations ([Galton, "Eugenics: Its Definition, Scope, and Aims"](https://sourcebooks.fordham.edu/mod/1904galton-eugenics.asp)).

That pair of documents was enough. I read them aloud over the dirt, one in the language of clinic websites, one in the language of imperial confidence, and the grave opened as if history had recognized its own accent.

Galton rose offended by the century. He brushed soil from his lapels, looked at the embryo dish in my hand, and smiled with the hideous satisfaction of a man discovering that the future has finally built his instrument.

You see, he said, I was early, not wrong.

That was the quarrel. The controversial question I had dug him up to face was simple to state and difficult to survive: should parents be allowed to select or genetically alter embryos not merely to avoid catastrophic disease, but to produce children with lower predicted risks, better traits, and more desirable futures? Not in the cartoon version, not blue eyes ordered from a touchscreen, but in the form that is already technically and commercially approaching us, embryo screening for more and more conditions, and, at the edge of the market, polygenic ranking based on statistical correlations whose confidence varies sharply across populations ([Nature Medicine, "Polygenic embryo screening: four clinical considerations"](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-021-01571-8)).

Galton loved the question immediately because it allowed him to pretend that coercive eugenics had died while its ambition had merely become polite. Why should rational parents not use available knowledge to improve the prospects of their children? If they already choose schools, diets, neighborhoods, books, and sometimes even whether to conceive at all, why should the genome alone be placed behind sacred glass? The old objection to eugenics, he said, was that the state had no right to breed citizens like cattle. Fine. Let the state step back. Let private choice do the work with a softer hand. Let the family become the laboratory in which civilization quietly corrects itself.

The ugliness of the argument is obvious, which is one reason it is dangerous. People think bad ideas announce themselves with a villain's laugh. Usually they arrive wearing the vocabulary of care. Galton was not asking whether we should exterminate the unfit. He was asking whether loving parents should refuse a tool that might give their child a lower risk of disease, a longer life, or better odds in a competitive world. Once framed that way, opposition begins to sound like sentimentality. And that, I think, is exactly where the trap is set.

I conceded the strongest point first, because otherwise the conversation becomes dishonest. There is a moral difference between preventing a child from inheriting a devastating monogenic disorder and ranking embryos by probabilistic scores for complex traits. The first is already part of ordinary reproductive medicine in some cases, and not absurdly. If a family knows it carries a mutation associated with Tay-Sachs or Huntington's disease, using PGT to avoid passing that condition on is not obviously an expression of contempt for humanity. It can be an expression of mercy ([HFEA, "Pre-implantation genetic testing (PGT)"](https://www.hfea.gov.uk/treatments/explore-all-treatments/pre-implantation-genetic-testing-pgt/); [National Academies, *Human Genome Editing: Science, Ethics, and Governance*](https://nap.nationalacademies.org/catalog/24623/human-genome-editing-science-ethics-and-governance)). To deny that is to confuse moral seriousness with theatrical purity.

Galton pounced on the concession. If disease prevention is allowed, he said, the principle is already gone. Every trait exists on a continuum with suffering somewhere in the story. Lower risk of heart disease. Lower risk of schizophrenia. Better impulse control. Better memory. Greater resistance to depression. Longer projected lifespan. Even educational attainment can be redescribed as a health-adjacent good if one is sufficiently bourgeois about despair. The difference between therapy and enhancement, he argued, is not a wall but a border drawn in chalk by people embarrassed by their own desires.

He was not entirely wrong. That is what makes him so poisonous. Once reproduction is reorganized around optimization, the logic does not like to stop where ethicists would prefer it to stop. Markets are excellent solvents of moral distinctions. A clinic that begins with avoiding severe disease does not need to announce a eugenic program in order to slide toward embryo comparison as premium consumer service. It needs only anxious parents, competitive inequality, and a sales pitch. Modern genomics already tempts that slide because polygenic scores can be presented as sober statistics rather than fantasies, even when the underlying predictive power is partial, population-specific, and easily overstated ([Nature Medicine, "Polygenic embryo screening: four clinical considerations"](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-021-01571-8)).

But I told him he was still cheating. He wanted inevitability where there was only pressure. The fact that a line is difficult to hold does not mean it is meaningless. We make hard distinctions all the time because civilization depends on refusing easy continuities. We distinguish surgery from mutilation, taxation from theft, consent from coercion, palliative sedation from homicide. Those distinctions are contested, leaky, and vulnerable to abuse, but abolishing them because they are not metaphysically perfect would be civilizational laziness.

The better question, I said, is not whether selection and editing form a seamless spectrum. The better question is what moral posture toward the future child is being enacted. When parents use reproductive technology to avoid a severe and well-characterized disease, they are trying, at least in the best case, to prevent a specific catastrophe. When they rank embryos by projected intelligence, height, mood stability, or composite life-success proxies, they are not preventing catastrophe. They are converting the child into a speculative asset. They are deciding that existence should begin not with welcome, but with benchmarking.

Galton replied that this was pious nonsense. Every society benchmarks children. Some do it crudely by inheritance and schooling; some will do it more efficiently by genetics. He thought I was sentimental about the old forms of inequality because they lacked laboratory glassware. And then he made his most uncomfortable argument. If affluent parents can already buy safer neighborhoods, better prenatal care, tutors, legacy admissions, and social networks, why should genetic selection be singled out as uniquely corrupt? Is it not merely one more advantage among many, and potentially a more truthful one because it acts earlier?

I hated the force of that question because it exposes a common hypocrisy. Liberal societies are often willing to denounce futuristic forms of inequality while tolerating the inherited ones that structure ordinary life. But the answer is not that embryo selection is therefore acceptable. The answer is that it concentrates and naturalizes injustice in a uniquely intimate way. Social inequalities can at least be named as political arrangements. A child selected, ranked, or edited for marketable advantage enters a world in which hierarchy has been folded into the story of their body before they can speak. The injustice no longer appears contingent. It begins to masquerade as merit at the biological level.

That matters because bodies carry metaphysics. Galton's entire program depended on making social judgments look like natural facts. Once a society starts sorting embryos according to preferred traits, it does not simply distribute benefits unevenly. It teaches itself which kinds of people count as regrettable risks, which kinds of minds are premium goods, and which forms of dependency are design failures. That is why disability critics have long warned that reproductive selection can express, even when motivated by love, a background judgment that some lives are less welcome or less worth preparing a world for. The political baseline should be the equal dignity and rights of persons with disabilities, not the fantasy that justice is best achieved by screening them out in advance ([United Nations, Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities](https://www.un.org/disabilities/documents/convention/convoptprot-e.pdf)).

Galton, who had no use for dignity unless it improved a pedigree chart, shrugged. He said rights talk is what decadent societies invent when they have lost the courage to choose excellence.

There it was, finally, the clean brutality underneath the contemporary euphemisms. Excellence for whom? Chosen by whom? According to what image of the good life? A state that orders genetic outcomes is monstrous. But a market that rewards them can become monstrous by diffusion, without ever issuing a decree. Parents do not need to hate disabled people to participate in a system that steadily narrows the range of lives regarded as acceptable. They need only feel responsible, frightened, and surrounded by competitors.

I asked him whether he understood what the twentieth century had done with his ideas. He said of course he did, and he despised amateurism. That answer chilled me more than denial would have. He was too intelligent to rely on innocence. He preferred salvage. He wanted to rescue the ambition of directed human improvement from the crimes committed in its name.

This is exactly why the debate cannot be settled by saying, as many people do, that modern reproductive genetics is not eugenics because it is voluntary. Voluntary for whom? Under what pressures? In what market? With what background inequalities? Consent is morally important, but it is not a magic detergent. A practice can be chosen and still express a social order that trains people to choose against solidarity. Galton's ghost was right about one thing: coercion is not the only engine of eugenic thinking. Aspiration can do a lot of the work.

We turned then to gene editing itself, the more dramatic frontier. The birth of CRISPR-edited babies in China was widely condemned not only because the intervention was medically reckless, but because the governance and ethical justification were indefensible ([Cyranoski, *Nature*, "The CRISPR-baby scandal"](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-019-00673-1)). International bodies have urged extreme caution or restraint precisely because heritable genome editing reaches beyond one patient to future persons who cannot consent and to social consequences no clinic can fully contain ([UNESCO International Bioethics Committee, *Report of the IBC on Updating Its Reflection on the Human Genome and Human Rights*](https://unesdoc.unesco.org/ark:/48223/pf0000373088)). Galton, naturally, found the scandal provincial. He thought the real obscenity was possessing a tool and using it timidly.

I told him timidity was not the issue. Humility was. To intervene in the germline is not merely to cure; it is to legislate biologically for people who must live inside decisions they did not make. The parent-child relation is already unequal. It should not become proprietary. There is a moral difference between raising a child and authoring one. Even where editing might one day be safe enough to prevent grave disease, a just society would need public rules narrow enough to prevent the technology from becoming a sorting machine for status competition ([National Academies, *Human Genome Editing: Science, Ethics, and Governance*](https://nap.nationalacademies.org/catalog/24623/human-genome-editing-science-ethics-and-governance)).

Galton's answer was severe and, in a way, clarifying. He said that if my society really believed children were not projects, it would not already treat them as investments from preschool onward. He thought our moral panic about genetics was selective. We object when ambition becomes molecular because the mirror gets too accurate.

I could not dismiss that completely. He was naming a real corruption. But he mistook diagnosis for vindication. The fact that we already instrumentalize children is an argument to stop, not a reason to engineer the process more efficiently. Technology does not become innocent because it resembles our existing sins.

By then dawn was coming up hard and gray. The graveyard looked less gothic and more administrative. Galton began to sink, very slightly, as if the earth had opinions of its own.

So I told him the conclusion I had wanted to avoid because it requires drawing a line and then defending it in public against two hostile camps. The line is this. Reproductive technologies aimed at avoiding severe, clearly defined genetic disease can be morally justified under strict public regulation, medical necessity, transparency, and a political culture that refuses to treat disabled lives as disposable. But embryo selection for socially prized traits, and especially polygenic ranking for competitive advantage, should be prohibited. Heritable gene editing, if ever permitted at all, should be confined to exceptional therapeutic cases and governed as a matter of public ethics, not boutique preference.

Why this line? Because justice is not the same thing as optimization. Because the child is not a product category. Because equality dies not only when the state commands breeding, but when the market persuades families to internalize the breeder's gaze. Because a society worthy of new people should fight harder to widen the conditions under which many kinds of people can flourish, rather than narrowing the gateway through which only the preferred may enter.

Galton gave me one last look, half contempt and half delight, the look of a man pleased that his enemies are at least taking him seriously. Then he said the sentence I had raised him to hear and feared he would prove right about.

You will call it medicine, he said, and then you will call it responsibility, and then one day you will discover you have rebuilt me out of kindness.

Then he went down.

I left the grave carrying the embryo dish like a small accusation. The stakes are not abstract. They are political, commercial, familial, and civilizational all at once. They concern who gets welcomed, what burdens parents are told to carry, what inequalities are allowed to harden into biology, and whether the future human being arrives as a citizen or as a quality-controlled deliverable. That is why this argument matters so much. Galton is dead, which is good. The problem is that he no longer needs to call himself Galton to keep talking.
