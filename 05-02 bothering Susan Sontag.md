today is May 2, 2026.

I found Susan Sontag where serious readers leave their dead when they cannot bear to call them dead: in the annex stacks, below the living library, where the basement always smells faintly of wet cardboard and iron. There was no coffin. Only a long table of discarded review copies, a failed dehumidifier, and a box of books whose pages had furred at the edges from one bad winter. *Regarding the Pain of Others* lay there swollen with damp, as if the book itself had taken on water. When I opened it, the room changed its pressure. Dust rose, then held itself in the air. The fluorescent lights began that ugly insect hum particular to institutional afterlives. I do not know how resurrection works in theology. I only know that criticism, practiced with enough hunger, can become a necromancy. I read until the sentence became a threshold, and then Susan Sontag stood on the other side of the table, irritated to have been summoned by someone who meant it.

She was less ghostly than exact. Her face had the exhausted sharpness of someone already disappointed by the first question. I told her why I had brought her back. I did not want a séance about literature, nor a sentimental blessing for the camera. I wanted an argument about one of the most difficult editorial decisions a society can make: whether the public should be shown graphic images of the dead, especially dead children and civilians, in order to force moral attention. We have arguments about this every time atrocity becomes visible. Should newspapers print the body, the shredded face, the open casket, the drowned child on the beach? Or is that a second violation, a conversion of suffering into civic pornography?

Sontag looked, not at me, but at the table, where the damp book was still open. She had already asked versions of this question in her own work. In *Looking at War*, she returned to Virginia Woolf’s wager that photographs of mutilated bodies might produce a common revulsion at war, while doubting that images can by themselves secure either understanding or peace ([Sontag, *The New Yorker*, 2002](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2002/12/09/looking-at-war)). And after Abu Ghraib, she insisted that the photographs mattered precisely because they did not merely illustrate abuse but constituted evidence of a system that had become thinkable to its perpetrators ([Sontag, *The New York Times Magazine*, 2004](https://www.nytimes.com/2004/05/23/magazine/regarding-the-torture-of-others.html)). So I gave her the proposition in its harshest form: sometimes the dead must be seen in public, in their damaged condition, because otherwise the living keep lying.

She said my sentence was too eager, which was true. Eagerness is one of the disguises moral vanity wears.

The case for showing the dead is not hard to state, and that is one reason it attracts the self-righteous. Sometimes an image punctures euphemism more effectively than a thousand official statements. Mamie Till-Mobley chose an open casket for her fourteen-year-old son so that the public would be forced to see what racist murder had done to his body; as she put it, she wanted to “let the world see what has happened” ([History](https://www.history.com/articles/emmett-till)). The publication of the torture photographs from Abu Ghraib converted what might have remained bureaucratic abstraction into undeniable scandal; Seymour Hersh’s reporting, drawing on the Taguba report and the images themselves, documented “sadistic, blatant, and wanton criminal abuses” that the occupying power could no longer package as a few regrettable rumors ([Hersh, *The New Yorker*, 2004](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2004/05/10/torture-at-abu-ghraib)). The photograph of Alan Kurdi, the Syrian child whose body washed up on a Turkish beach, spread because editors and readers understood that a refugee crisis described in aggregate had become morally unserious; the image restored one irreplaceable body to a discourse otherwise built from nouns like “flows,” “burdens,” and “migrants” ([BBC, 2015](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-europe-34133210)).

That is the best argument for publication. Graphic images can perform an anti-abstract function. They can drag policy back down to flesh.

Sontag did not deny this. She distrusted cleaner lies more than dirty truth. But she objected to the leap from “images can reveal” to “therefore more revelation is better.” That leap, she said, mistakes disclosure for ethics. The question is not whether images can matter. The question is what kind of relation they install between spectator and suffering. In *Looking at War*, she warned against the lazy faith that showing horror automatically ennobles viewers, as if exposure itself were a civic sacrament ([Sontag, *The New Yorker*, 2002](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2002/12/09/looking-at-war)). A public can be informed by atrocity images, yes. It can also become trained by them, acquiring not courage but appetite, not solidarity but a taste for moral excitement. To look is not yet to understand. To understand is not yet to act. To act is not yet to act justly.

I told her this was true and still insufficient. The anti-publication argument often borrows the language of dignity while doing the practical work of suppression. States prefer decorous ignorance. Militaries love privacy for corpses. Politicians happily invoke respect for victims when what they really mean is protection for institutions. If editors decide that mutilation is too disturbing for breakfast readers, the beneficiaries are usually not the dead. They are the people who made the dead. This is why the moral vocabulary around “dignity” must be handled with suspicion. Dignity for whom? For the person killed, who can no longer authorize the image? For the family, who may or may not want witness? Or for the audience, who would rather preserve a self-image of decency than confront what its governments, armies, borders, police, or hatreds have done?

Sontag’s face hardened at the word “audience.” She said modern spectators always want innocence twice over: first from the violence, then from the looking. They want to consume evidence while denying complicity in the market for it. I had revived the right dead woman for this quarrel because she refused the cheap absolutions available on either side. She would not let the image be a pure instrument of truth; nor would she let refined discomfort masquerade as moral seriousness.

So we moved to the real controversy. Not whether atrocity images *can* be shown, but under what rule. I argued for a demanding presumption in favor of publication when three conditions hold. First, the image must reveal a matter of genuine public consequence, not merely satisfy curiosity. The abuse at Abu Ghraib met this standard because it exposed the operating culture of a prison run by an occupying army, not a private sorrow detached from power ([Hersh, *The New Yorker*, 2004](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2004/05/10/torture-at-abu-ghraib)). Second, the image must defeat active falsification or sentimental abstraction, as Emmett Till’s open casket defeated Southern euphemism and national distance ([History](https://www.history.com/articles/emmett-till)). Third, publication must be accompanied by language that identifies agents, structures, and stakes, so that the picture is not left to circulate as free-floating shock. An image without explanation can become merely a wound-shaped commodity.

Sontag said my rule was better than the common one, which is no rule at all, just a blend of traffic logic and institutional cowardice. But she thought it still trusted editors too much. Context, she said, is not a caption problem. It is a political problem. The same photograph can function as indictment, warning, trophy, fetish, or proof of tribal grievance depending on the ecology of its circulation. In a magazine essay, an image can be embedded in argument. On a platform optimized for speed, repetition, and emotional extraction, it enters a machine designed to flatten distinctions. The contemporary image economy does not merely show the dead. It distributes their remains according to engagement metrics.

That landed. The question is harsher now than it was in Woolf’s time or even in Sontag’s own. When an editor printed a photograph in a newspaper, the decision carried scarcity. Now every decision is made inside abundance, duplication, and algorithmic escalation. The public is not simply shown an image once. It is pursued by it, sorted by it, and taught by the machine which corpses are geopolitically legible. One dead child becomes iconic; ten thousand others remain numerically invisible. This is not an argument against publication. It is an argument against piety about publication. The image enters a hierarchy of grief the moment it is uploaded.

I said that if this is true, the answer cannot be retreat into taboo. A squeamish public is not a humane public. It may only be a more comfortable one. The people who insist that the dead must never be shown often mean that the living must never be forced. And force, in politics, matters. There are moments when moral attention has to be wrestled from indifference. That is why the Alan Kurdi photograph mattered even if its effects were partial and unstable: it interrupted the bureaucratic grammar in which mass drowning had become normal background noise ([BBC, 2015](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-europe-34133210)). A single image cannot redeem policy, but it can break a sentence the powerful have been repeating too easily.

Sontag answered with the coldest line of the night: interruption is not transformation. She was right, and I hated that she was right. Democracies are full of interrupted people who return, a day later, to the same appetites and alibis. Outrage is often just attention with theater makeup on. If the publication of atrocity images becomes a substitute for organization, law, journalism, and material risk, then the dead are being used to subsidize the conscience of spectators. The image says: feel this. Politics asks: what will you now give up?

There, finally, was the stake. The controversy is not about taste. It is about the terms under which the dead may compel the living. If we ban graphic witness, we make denial easier. If we celebrate graphic witness too easily, we make voyeurism noble. A serious ethics of showing the dead has to hold both thoughts at once. Publish when concealment would protect power, when the image bears on public wrongdoing, and when the subject has been dragged into history in a way that demands witness. But publish with friction: with names, causes, institutions, dates, limits, and an explicit refusal to turn pain into aesthetic atmosphere. Refuse the crop that beautifies. Refuse the headline that universalizes away responsibility. Refuse the smug editorial note about “raising awareness” when awareness is the least costly thing a viewer can possess.

Sontag seemed almost satisfied, which on her face looked like a reduction in contempt. She said that what I was really defending was not the right to show anything, but the duty to make looking harder. Yes. Exactly. The deepest obscenity is not the photograph. It is the consumption of the photograph under conditions engineered to dissolve memory, agency, and obligation. The editor’s task, then, is not to protect the public from horror. It is to prevent horror from being laundered into sensation.

The fluorescent lights flickered. Water bled a little farther into the table grain. Sontag was already receding, called back by whatever afterlife awaits the difficult. Before she went, she placed two fingers on the ruined copy of her book and told me not to trust anyone who speaks about “the power of images” in the abstract. Images do not have power like electricity has power. They have force only in arrangement: with sentences, institutions, audiences, and histories. The dead are not honored by being hidden, and they are not honored by being displayed. They are honored, if that word can still do any work here, when their visibility increases the cost of lying.

Then she was gone, and I was left in the basement with the embarrassing simplicity of the conclusion. Show the dead when not showing them would help the killers, the jailers, the bombers, the racists, the border regime, the state, or the comfortable liar. But never confuse the act of showing with the completion of justice. The photograph can open the door to conscience. It cannot walk through it for us.
