today is May 1, 2026.

I found Ivan Illich in Bremen, under rain that seemed too disciplined to count as weather. The grave was modest, almost corrective. That felt right. Illich spent too much of his life stripping prestige off institutions to want any granite sermon over his bones. I had come because the old argument about death has become new again, and uglier. In Canada, Belgium, the Netherlands, and a growing circle of legal imagination, the question is no longer merely whether a terminally ill person may ask for help dying. The harder question is whether a society should authorize medical aid in dying for people whose suffering is psychiatric, or for people who have written advance directives asking to be euthanized after severe dementia has erased the self that wrote them. The stakes are not semantic. They are whether autonomy survives cognitive ruin, whether medicine heals by helping some people die, and whether a compassionate state can tell the difference between mercy and abandonment.

I woke Illich by laying medical documents on the stone like accusations. I read from the Supreme Court of Canada’s decision in *Carter v. Canada*, which held that the prohibition on physician-assisted death unjustifiably infringed the rights of competent adults suffering intolerably from grievous and irremediable medical conditions ([*Carter v. Canada*, 2015 SCC 5](https://scc-csc.lexum.com/scc-csc/scc-csc/en/item/14637/index.do)). I read from the Government of Canada’s current description of MAID eligibility, including the delayed but still live plan to expand eligibility to cases where mental illness is the sole underlying medical condition ([Government of Canada, “Medical assistance in dying”](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/health-services-benefits/medical-assistance-dying.html)). Then I read from the Dutch Regional Euthanasia Review Committees, which explicitly discuss euthanasia based on advance directives in patients with severe dementia ([RTE Netherlands, “Advance euthanasia directive”](https://english.euthanasiecommissie.nl/the-committees/advance-euthanasia-directive)). The wet earth moved a little. Illich rose like a man irritated to discover that the clinic had followed him underground.

He looked at the papers and said, almost cheerfully, “You have made dying into a professional service.”

That was the opening blow, and it landed. Illich’s great theme in *Medical Nemesis* was that modern medicine does not merely cure, it expands into a cultural monopoly that teaches people to experience birth, pain, dependence, and death only through institutional management ([Ivan Illich, *Medical Nemesis*](https://archive.org/details/medicalnemesisex00illi)). He thought industrial medicine often generated its own forms of harm, not only clinical side effects but what he called “cultural iatrogenesis,” the theft of ordinary human capacities to suffer, care, and die. From that angle, medical aid in dying looks suspicious from the start. A society takes existential terror, family burden, dependency, despair, and the loss of self, and routes them into a licensed procedure. Even mercy becomes something billed, regulated, certified.

I told him that this critique is powerful and incomplete. It is one thing to say medicine colonizes life. It is another to tell a lucid person in agony that the only morally clean death is an unmanaged one. The modern defense of assisted dying is not mainly a worship of technique. At its best, it is a claim about sovereignty over one’s own body and biography. Ronald Dworkin argued that the value at stake in end-of-life decisions is not mere preference satisfaction but the integrity of a life as a whole, including a person’s right to shape the final chapter in line with their deepest convictions about dignity and meaning ([Ronald Dworkin, *Life’s Dominion*](https://archive.org/details/lifesdominionarg00dwor)). If someone judges that continued existence under certain conditions would violate the shape they want their life to have had, why should the state force them to endure it?

Illich gave me a look I have only ever seen on priests and anarchists when they suspect the same corruption from opposite directions. He said the word *sovereignty* was doing dishonest work. Autonomy is not a pure light floating above institutions. It is socially manufactured, pressured, frightened, and taught what to want. A patient does not choose in a vacuum. She chooses within a medical culture, an insurance system, a family economy, a hierarchy of dependence, and a civilization that increasingly treats needing care as humiliation. If you make death an option inside that system, he said, it will not remain a last resort for the rare lucid tragic case. It will become a signal. Some lives are burdensome enough that the compassionate thing is to let them end.

That is not paranoia. It is the strongest argument against expanding assisted dying. Disability scholars and advocates have been making versions of it for years. The United Nations Special Rapporteur on the rights of persons with disabilities warned in 2019 that legal regimes for assisted dying can rest on, and reinforce, assumptions that disability or dependence make life less worth living ([UN OHCHR, Report of the Special Rapporteur on the rights of persons with disabilities, 2019](https://documents.un.org/doc/undoc/gen/g19/100/34/pdf/g1910034.pdf)). The concern is not that every request for assisted death is false consciousness. The concern is that legal permission operates in a moral atmosphere already saturated with ableism, loneliness, underfunded care, and panic about being a burden.

I pressed back because the anti-MAID case often cheats by acting as though all vulnerability invalidates choice. We let vulnerable people refuse treatment. We let them sign DNR orders. We accept, at least in law, that a competent person may decline life-sustaining intervention even when death will predictably follow ([American Medical Association, Code of Medical Ethics 5.3, “Withholding or Withdrawing Life-Sustaining Treatment”](https://code-medical-ethics.ama-assn.org/ethics-opinions/withholding-or-withdrawing-life-sustaining-treatment)). That boundary matters. If bodily integrity means anything, it must include the right not to be dragooned into continued existence by medical power. The question is why assistance in dying should be categorically different if the person’s judgment is competent, persistent, informed, and directed at escaping intolerable suffering.

Illich answered that refusing treatment and requesting death are not morally identical. In the first case, medicine ceases to interfere. In the second, medicine becomes the designated executioner of a judgment about which lives should end now. He was not impressed by the euphemism of assistance. “The hand remains the hand,” he said. “Only the rhetoric becomes cleaner.”

That objection is morally serious, but it can be overstated. The line between killing and letting die has never been as stable as moralists pretend. James Rachels argued half a century ago that the bare distinction often fails to track what actually matters, since motives, consent, and suffering can make a passive death no cleaner than an active one ([James Rachels, “Active and Passive Euthanasia,” *New England Journal of Medicine*](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJM197501093020206)). If a physician may sedate a patient into unconsciousness while withdrawing treatment, foreseeing death, the intuition that direct assistance is uniquely contaminated can start to look like metaphysical squeamishness rather than moral clarity.

Illich did not deny the force of that point. He shifted terrain, which was wise. “You are still asking the easier question,” he said. “A competent dying patient is tragic. But at least there is a speaker. Your century is now asking whether the earlier self may sentence the later self to death.”

That was the true subject, and the rain seemed to tighten around it. Advance directives for dementia are where end-of-life liberalism loses its easy footing. The person who writes, while fully competent, that she does not want to live once she no longer recognizes her children or can no longer form coherent reasons, is making a claim about identity across time. Dworkin defended the moral authority of such directives by distinguishing a person’s “critical interests,” which express what makes a life successful or faithful to one’s values, from merely experiential interests, such as present pleasures or comforts ([Ronald Dworkin, *Life’s Dominion*](https://archive.org/details/lifesdominionarg00dwor)). On that view, a later contented but severely demented self does not simply overrule the earlier person’s considered judgment about what kind of existence would betray the life she meant to live.

I told Illich I find that argument both brilliant and frightening. Brilliant because it honors the seriousness of biography. Frightening because it can sound like a refined permission to kill a smiling person for the sake of a no-longer-present authorial self. Rebecca Dresser has argued that the precedent autonomy model gives too much authority to the earlier competent self and too little moral weight to the welfare and interests of the later incompetent patient who still exists and can still experience the world ([Rebecca Dresser, “Dworkin on Dementia,” *The Hastings Center Report*](https://www.jstor.org/stable/3528622)). If the present person enjoys music, cake, sunlight, touch, or simply the brute animal ease of an untroubled afternoon, it is not obvious that the vanished philosopher who once despised dependency should get the final vote.

Illich surprised me by refusing the easy sanctity-of-life line. He did not say every demented life must be prolonged at all costs. He hated compulsory treatment too much for that. Instead, he said that dementia exposes the arrogance hidden inside many autonomy arguments. Liberal adults imagine themselves as authors. Then age arrives and reveals that persons are also creatures, porous and discontinuous, often unable to preserve the narrative command they mistook for essence. To insist that the earlier planner absolutely governs the later dependent being is not always dignity. It can be disgust with creatureliness dressed as principle.

That sentence annoyed me because it explained too much. There is, undeniably, a horror in dementia that is not reducible to prejudice. Neurodegenerative disease can involve terror, agitation, loss of recognition, incontinence, and the collapse of practical agency. The National Institute on Aging describes dementia as progressive loss affecting memory, language, problem-solving, and daily function, with symptoms that can become profound ([National Institute on Aging, “What Is Dementia?”](https://www.nia.nih.gov/health/alzheimers-and-dementia/what-dementia-symptoms-types-and-diagnosis)). People are not irrational to fear such an end. But fear of that end easily recruits a fantasy that the self is only valuable while articulate, independent, and architecturally intact. That fantasy is morally poisonous.

So I made the best case I could for a narrow pro-authority view of advance directives. Suppose a person, while competent, repeatedly and explicitly states that once severe dementia destroys recognition, deliberation, and the projects constitutive of her identity, she does not want to continue living. Suppose she understands alternatives, has access to good palliative and social care, is not being coerced, and has held the view over time. Suppose, further, that later she reaches exactly that condition, with no sign of present resistance. Why should the law treat her earlier self as a stranger? We honor past selves in contracts, marriages, refusals of treatment, wills, and organ donation. Why become metaphysically agnostic only when the issue is death?

Illich answered that wills dispose of property. They do not extinguish a presently breathing being under institutional supervision. He also noted what defenders of euthanasia sometimes glide past. The Dutch system, often cited as the most experienced regime, treats euthanasia for advanced dementia as exceptional and difficult precisely because assessing unbearable suffering and voluntariness becomes morally and clinically fraught when reciprocal communication is compromised ([RTE Netherlands, “Dementia”](https://english.euthanasiecommissie.nl/topics/dementia)). The fact that some jurisdictions permit the practice does not dissolve the epistemic problem. When the current patient cannot renew, clarify, or withdraw the old instruction in the robust way liberal legality likes, other people begin interpreting her life on her behalf. Families do. Physicians do. Committees do. States do. The dead hand of precedent autonomy is guided by many living hands.

He was right, and the practical evidence cuts both ways. Oversight reports and parliamentary reviews in multiple countries show that safeguards around assisted dying are not fantasy, but they also reveal recurring disputes about trackability, assessment quality, and social conditions surrounding requests ([Government of Canada, Fifth Annual Report on Medical Assistance in Dying in Canada, 2024](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/publications/health-system-services/annual-report-medical-assistance-dying-2023.html); [House of Lords Select Committee on the Assisted Dying for the Terminally Ill Bill, 2005](https://publications.parliament.uk/pa/ld200405/ldselect/ldasdy/86/8601.htm)). Serious people can read the same record and see either humane pluralism or a slow normalization of death as service delivery.

By then the argument had become sharper than policy. It was about what respect for persons requires when personhood itself has dimmed or fractured. I told Illich I reject the lazy pieties on both sides. It is false that every assisted death is abandonment. It is also false that legalizing more options automatically expands freedom. A choice offered inside abandonment can be a command in polite clothing. If a society will fund lethal injections faster than home care, palliative care, disability support, psychiatric treatment, or respite for families, then its language of compassion is morally rotten. The World Health Organization has been blunt that palliative care remains seriously underprovided globally, even though it is an ethical responsibility of health systems ([World Health Organization, “Palliative care”](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/palliative-care)). A state that neglects care while refining death has not become humane. It has become efficient.

That was the first thing Illich heard from me that he seemed to trust. He said the central political question is not merely whether some individuals should be permitted assistance in dying. It is what social world makes that option appear necessary, noble, or merciful. If people seek death because pain relief is unavailable, because disability support is humiliatingly thin, because housing is precarious, because loneliness has become chronic, or because they have absorbed the lesson that dependency cancels dignity, then legalization can become an anesthetic over structural failure.

I agreed, but refused his temptation toward purity. There are still cases, even in a more just society, where a competent person’s enduring wish to die should be honored. I would defend medical aid in dying for competent adults under strict criteria where suffering is grievous and irremediable, alternatives have been meaningfully explored, and social deprivation is not masquerading as consent. *Carter* was right, I think, that forcing some people to endure intolerable existence can violate liberty and security in a profound sense ([*Carter v. Canada*, 2015 SCC 5](https://scc-csc.lexum.com/scc-csc/scc-csc/en/item/14637/index.do)). But I would be far more reluctant about two expansions: cases where mental illness alone is the basis for eligibility, and cases where a past directive is used to end the life of a person now living with advanced dementia.

Why the reluctance? Because both contexts radically intensify the two deepest problems in assisted dying: whether the choice is genuinely stable and autonomous, and whether the present social world has earned the moral authority to cooperate in death. Psychiatric suffering is real, sometimes catastrophic, and not less real because its mechanisms are mental. But prognostic uncertainty, suicidality as symptom, and the entanglement of despair with reversible social conditions make error especially haunting there. Severe dementia creates a different terror: the metaphysical split between the earlier valuing self and the later vulnerable being. In both contexts, society’s confidence should drop, not rise.

Illich’s final attack came as the rain thinned. He said my position still trusted institutions too much. I still wanted the right committee, the right criteria, the right ethical vocabulary, the right doctor with the right forms. Perhaps. But I told him there is no exit from institutions, only better and worse ones. The real moral test is whether they are arranged around care first, death second, and never death as a substitute for care.

He faded then, not with grandeur but with the mild exasperation of a man leaving a meeting that had gone on too long. I stood in the rain and kept the verdict he had forced out of me.

A decent society may, in some cases, permit medical aid in dying for competent adults. But it should do so with moral suspicion toward itself, not congratulation. It should never present assisted death as the flagship of progress while basic care remains rationed or undignified. And it should be extraordinarily cautious, perhaps prohibitive, where the request depends on precedent autonomy through severe dementia or on psychiatric suffering alone. In those zones the risk is not only mistaken diagnosis or weak safeguards. It is that we flatter ourselves that we are honoring freedom when we are partly evacuating our obligations to the dependent, the difficult, the despairing, and the cognitively altered.

The controversial truth, I think, is that both camps often lie about what they fear. Opponents of assisted dying sometimes fear loss of sacred control over death but disguise it as universal principle. Supporters sometimes fear helplessness, mess, and dependence, and disguise that fear as dignity. Illich, to my annoyance, made me admit that the second fear has seeped deep into modern moral language. Not all requests to die express it, but our civilization has made that fear unusually articulate.

So I left him buried and kept the harder sentence. The question is not whether autonomy matters at the end of life. It does. The question is whether we love autonomy so badly that we would rather authorize death than learn how to accompany forms of life that wound our ideals of self-possession. Any society answering that question should do so with its hands shaking.
