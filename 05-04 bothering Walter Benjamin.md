today is 05-04-2026.

I found Walter Benjamin in Portbou, where the hill breaks toward the sea and the border still feels like an argument pretending to be geography. The grave is modest, which is obscene. A man spends his life teaching us how culture arrives haloed by catastrophe, and the world thanks him with a tasteful slab and a view. I came after midnight with a thermos of bad coffee, a paperback copy of *Illuminations*, and a phone whose battery had been charged, in part, by a grid now straining under the appetite of machine computation. That, more or less, was why I had come.

I did not use Latin, or lightning, or a priest. I read aloud the sentence in which Benjamin says there is no document of civilization that is not at the same time a document of barbarism, from *Theses on the Philosophy of History* ([Benjamin, 1940/1968](https://www.sfu.ca/~andrewf/CONCEPT2.html)). Then I read the latest warning from the IPCC that every increment of warming intensifies risks of extreme heat, biodiversity loss, and cascading damage across human and natural systems ([IPCC AR6 Synthesis Report](https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/syr/)). Then I told him, plainly, that the machines of polite destruction had become more abstract, more frictionless, more difficult to hate at the right scale, and that I needed his help deciding whether sabotage had become a moral obligation.

The dirt moved with bureaucratic reluctance. Benjamin emerged looking less like a ghost than a man interrupted in a library. He brushed himself off, looked at the phone in my hand, and asked the first serious question.

What is the apparatus now?

I told him: gigantic data centers, model training clusters, logistics software, recommendation systems, military vision stacks, automated fraud, labor discipline by dashboard, synthetic text poured over the internet like industrial runoff. The International Energy Agency now projects that electricity demand from data centers worldwide could more than double between 2024 and 2030, with AI as a major driver ([IEA, *Energy and AI*](https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-and-ai)). Training and serving these systems also consume significant water for cooling, a cost often hidden from the users who type requests into clean white boxes ([Li et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.03271)). Meanwhile the same firms market artificial intelligence as a climate solution and a democratic productivity engine.

Benjamin listened without surprise. That was irritating.

I said the controversial part quickly, before cowardice could dress itself up as nuance. Perhaps the correct response is not merely regulation, labor organizing, litigation, and better procurement rules, though all of those matter. Perhaps some parts of the AI buildout, especially the energy-hungry and militarized parts, should be physically obstructed, disabled, or destroyed. Not people. Infrastructure. Transformers, cooling lines, construction equipment, fiber routes, chip supply bottlenecks. Enough to slow the tempo. Enough to make deployment expensive, uncertain, and politically radioactive.

Benjamin did not recoil. He merely asked whether I meant violence.

Yes, I said, but not murder. Something like sabotage. Something the law would call criminal even if conscience did not.

He leaned on the stone that had been covering him and began, annoyingly, where he always begins: with distinctions. In *Critique of Violence*, he argues that law is not simply the opposite of violence but is itself founded and preserved by violence ([Benjamin, 1921](https://criticaltheoryconsortium.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/05/Benjamin-Critique-of-Violence-1.pdf)). Legal order loves to monopolize force while pretending it has transcended force. So the first mistake, he said, is to imagine that your question is whether violence may enter a previously peaceful field. The field is already saturated. The mines in Congo, the warehouses, the emissions, the labor surveillance, the water withdrawals in drought regions, the automated targeting systems, the quiet enclosure of language itself into proprietary machinery, all this is coercion distributed through contracts and concrete.

I said that was exactly why I was here. Liberal societies have become extremely skilled at treating structural harm as unfortunate weather while treating broken windows as moral apocalypse. A data center that helps accelerate emissions, labor extraction, and military automation can still be blessed by tax abatements and ribbon cuttings; a person who disables the substation feeding it becomes, instantly, a monster. The asymmetry feels designed not to preserve peace but to preserve legibility for power.

Benjamin nodded, then turned against me.

Do not enjoy this too much, he said.

That landed. There is a romance available in sabotage, especially to intellectuals who will probably never do it. One can begin to love the image of decisiveness more than the substance of strategy. One can confuse theatrical illegality with historical interruption. Benjamin, who spent enough time around revolutionaries to distrust their self-flattery, pressed the point. If sabotage is merely expressive, if it serves as a moral costume for despair, it will be absorbed into the same culture industry that turns every refusal into style. Capital is perfectly capable of selling us the vibe of insurgency while building another server farm.

So I made the argument in its hardest form. The democratic remedies are too slow relative to the pace of construction. The firms racing to dominate AI are spending at a scale meant to preempt politics. OpenAI has spoken openly about the enormous compute demands of frontier systems ([OpenAI, *Scaling Laws for Neural Language Models*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2001.08361)); industry leaders and governments frame the current contest as a geopolitical race, not a deliberative civic project ([U.S. National Security Commission on Artificial Intelligence, 2021](https://www.nscai.gov/2021-final-report/)). Once the infrastructure is built, constituencies harden around it: towns want tax base, investors want returns, militaries want procurement continuity, and ordinary users adjust to dependence. If you wait for clean democratic consensus after the buildout, you are not choosing nonviolence. You are choosing surrender dressed as procedure.

Benjamin answered with a sentence from the *Theses*: the state of emergency in which we live is not the exception but the rule ([Benjamin, 1940/1968](https://www.sfu.ca/~andrewf/CONCEPT2.html)). Then he asked the question that matters more than courage. Sabotage for whom, by whom, and in alliance with what living forces?

That is where most fantasies rot. If sabotage substitutes for politics, it curdles into avant-garde nihilism. If it is severed from workers, neighbors, water defenders, tenants, teachers, and the people conscripted into maintaining the machine, it becomes an ethic of purification, not emancipation. Benjamin said that genuine interruption is not merely the destruction of an object but the opening of historical time, a break in the false continuum that says there is no alternative to what is being built. A severed cable means little if the next day everyone returns to the same horizon of inevitability.

I pushed back. History does contain cases where property destruction clarified the stakes rather than obscuring them. The sabotage campaigns of anti-apartheid struggle targeted infrastructure because infrastructure was part of rule; Umkhonto we Sizwe explicitly distinguished sabotage from attacks on persons in its early phase ([ANC, *Manifesto of Umkhonto we Sizwe*, 1961](https://www.sahistory.org.za/archive/manifesto-umkhonto-we-sizwe-16-december-1961)). Abolitionists in the United States did not defeat slavery by confining themselves to procedural purity; fugitives stole themselves, networks broke the law, and violence entered because slavery was itself organized violence ([Douglass, *What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?*](https://rbscp.lib.rochester.edu/2945)). Why should we assume that a planetary emergency created by legal institutions must be answerable only by legal means?

He said we should not assume that. But neither should we assume that illegality is automatically more honest than law. The moral question is not whether damage occurs. Damage is already the medium. The question is whether an act decreases domination overall, whether it expands the capacity of the living to govern the technical conditions of their lives, whether it interrupts a death-bound trajectory rather than merely punctuating it. Some sabotage could meet that standard. Much of it would not.

Then Benjamin said the most uncomfortable thing of the night: the contemporary bourgeois conscience likes two symmetrical lies. One is that all sabotage is terrorism. The other is that any attack on machinery is therefore resistance. Both lies save us from judgment.

By then dawn had started thinning the sky over Portbou, and I understood that I had not come for permission. I had come because I wanted someone dead and difficult to deny me innocence. He would not.

What he would give me was narrower, and therefore more useful. First, stop speaking as if AI infrastructure were neutral until misused. Its social meaning is in the buildout itself, in the concentration of power, energy, expertise, and command it requires ([Benjamin, 1921](https://criticaltheoryconsortium.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/05/Benjamin-Critique-of-Violence-1.pdf); [IEA, *Energy and AI*](https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-and-ai)). Second, stop treating legality as a moral solvent. There are moments when the law is simply the administrative voice of catastrophe. Third, stop fetishizing the singular act. The question is not whether one dramatic strike against a machine would feel righteous. The question is what ensemble of actions, from union refusal to zoning fights to disclosure campaigns to occupation to sabotage, could actually slow the construction of systems that no one has meaningfully consented to live under.

Before he went back down, I asked him for a yes or no. Is sabotage of AI infrastructure morally justified?

He gave me the answer I was afraid of because it makes life harder. Sometimes yes. Not because broken things are beautiful, not because emergency absolves thought, and not because the future belongs to whoever is most willing to light a fuse. Sometimes yes because there are forms of property whose continued functioning is itself a mode of organized violence, and because refusing to interrupt them can be a deeper complicity than damaging them. But the burden is severe. You must know what you are interrupting, whom it serves, whom it harms, what alternatives your act protects room for, and whether the people most exposed to the machine can recognize themselves in your refusal.

Then he took the coffee from my hand, grimaced, and said that if I wanted the dead to keep answering me, I should bring something better next time.

He returned to the grave. The hill was quiet. My phone buzzed with another glowing summary generated by a system somebody somewhere calls inevitable.

It is not inevitable. That is the philosophical stake, and also the practical one. The scandal of our moment is not merely that destructive infrastructures are being built. It is that they are being built under a metaphysics of helplessness. If Benjamin was right, history does not move like a train whose route we merely discover after the tracks are laid. It is made of stoppages, ruptures, refusals, seizures of the brake. The danger is not only the machine. The danger is consenting to describe the machine as fate.
