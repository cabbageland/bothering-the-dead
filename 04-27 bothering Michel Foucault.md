today is April 27, 2026.

I found Michel Foucault in the catacombs beneath Paris, where the dead are stacked with a bureaucrat's efficiency and a heretic's irony. Miles of femurs, miles of skulls, a republic of remains labeled by tunnel markers and municipal order. It seemed the right place to retrieve him, because nobody wrote more darkly and more precisely about the marriage between classification and power. If you want to ask whether medicine can ever stop being a soft police force, you should not summon a saint. You should summon a man who distrusted every institution that claims to save you by first describing you.

I brought a battery lantern, a folding chair, and a paper hospital wristband stolen from no hospital, only bought online because the internet sells symbols faster than the state can consecrate them. I looped the band around a tibia and wrote his name on it. That felt like the proper sacrament for this question. The dead, if they are to return, should be returned through the mechanism they spent their lives dissecting. I read aloud from his own warning that the modern state couples totalizing and individualizing power, managing populations while fastening itself to particular souls ([Michel Foucault, "Omnes et Singulatim: Toward a Criticism of Political Reason"](https://foucault.info/documents/foucault.omnesEtSingulatim.en/)). Then I asked the catacombs for one temporary violation of natural law.

The bone pile loosened. A man climbed out of the geometry of the dead brushing calcium dust from his collar like chalk from a blackboard. He looked annoyed, which was reassuring. Resurrections should not produce optimism. They should produce contempt sharpened into attention.

I told him the subject immediately. Should adults be required to obtain a psychiatric diagnosis, or some formal mental-health gatekeeping, before they can access gender-affirming hormones and surgeries? This is one of those modern disputes in which every side uses the language of protection and half the people involved are lying about what exactly they want protected. Some want to protect vulnerable patients from irreversible harm. Some want to protect doctors from legal and moral recklessness. Some want to protect trans adults from humiliating institutional delay. Some want, less nobly, to protect the old monopoly of the normal. The stakes are not decorative. They are whether an adult may alter a body on the authority of their own declared life, or whether the state and its licensed interpreters may insist on a diagnostic confession first.

Foucault gave a small smile that was not friendly. "So," he said, "you have built a system in which a person must narrate themselves persuasively enough to institutions before they are allowed to become legible to themselves." He did not need time to locate the nerve. In *The History of Sexuality, Volume I*, he argued that modern power does not merely repress sex, it incites people to produce ever more discourse about themselves, especially confessional discourse, under the sign of truth ([Michel Foucault, *The History of Sexuality, Volume I*](https://monoskop.org/images/a/a4/Foucault_Michel_The_History_of_Sexuality_1_An_Introduction_1990.pdf)). The clinic, he said, inherited the church's appetite. It no longer asks, "What sins have you committed?" It asks, "What are you, really?" and then files the answer.

His opening case was fierce. Requiring diagnosis before care, he said, does not simply identify distress. It produces a script. It teaches patients which words unlock treatment, which childhood memories sound authentic, which ambiguities must be hidden, which forms of doubt count as danger. The institution pretends to discover a truth already inside the patient, but in practice it trains the performance through which that truth becomes billable. The result is not honesty. It is disciplined narrative.

That is not fantasy. Even the American Psychiatric Association's own explanation of gender dysphoria reflects the tightrope: transgender identity itself is not the pathology, it says, and DSM-5 replaced "gender identity disorder" with "gender dysphoria" in order to focus on distress rather than identity, precisely because pathologizing identity had become untenable ([American Psychiatric Association, "Gender Dysphoria Diagnosis"](https://www.psychiatry.org/psychiatrists/diversity/education/transgender-and-gender-nonconforming-patients/gender-dysphoria-diagnosis)). But once access to hormones, surgeries, insurance coverage, and legal defensibility are routed through that diagnosis, the category does more than describe suffering. It governs passage.

I told him that this criticism lands, but it does not finish the question. Bodies are not essays. Hormones alter fertility, sexual function, bone health, cardiovascular risk profiles, and long-term medical needs. Surgeries can relieve misery and also permanently remove possibilities. If medicine is to remain medicine rather than retail desire, clinicians need some structure for judging capacity, coercion, comorbid instability, and whether the patient understands the risks. The Endocrine Society's guideline does not treat this as ornamental bureaucracy. It says diagnosing clinicians and mental health providers should be knowledgeable about diagnostic criteria and ongoing care, and that hormone treatment is not a casual one-click commodity ([Endocrine Society, "Gender Dysphoria/Gender Incongruence Guideline Resources"](https://www.endocrine.org/clinical-practice-guidelines/gender-dysphoria-gender-incongruence)). A civilization that refuses all gatekeeping in the name of liberation will eventually discover that it has liberated malpractice first.

He disliked that word, gatekeeping, because it concedes the architecture too quickly. Gates imply a castle whose owners are entitled to decide entry. He said the better question is who has been granted the authority to interpret another person's avowed relation to their own body. The modern clinic's genius, in his view, is that it presents political judgments as therapeutic caution. A patient comes seeking transformation. The institution replies that it only seeks safety. But safety is never innocent. In *Omnes et Singulatim*, Foucault describes pastoral power as a form that individualizes, surveys, and claims benevolence while binding obedience to salvation ([Foucault, "Omnes et Singulatim"](https://foucault.info/documents/foucault.omnesEtSingulatim.en/)). The psychiatrist, he implied, is not a prison guard. Worse. The psychiatrist is a guard who gets to call the arrangement care.

I answered that this was too pure, and purity is a luxury bought with somebody else's complications. There is a reason even relatively liberal protocols do not reduce everything to self-declaration. The UCSF Gender Affirming Health Program, which is much friendlier to informed-consent models than older systems were, still says providers must assess capacity for informed consent, meaning the ability to understand risks, benefits, alternatives, unknowns, and the risks of no treatment; it notes that many experienced clinics initiate hormones without a prior mental-health referral, but not without clinical assessment ([UCSF, "Initiating hormone therapy"](https://transcare.ucsf.edu/guidelines/initiating-hormone-therapy)). That distinction matters. The choice is not only between paternalistic diagnosis and libertarian vending machine. There is a middle ground in which adults are trusted with their lives but medicine still takes the concept of informed consent seriously.

Foucault pounced on the phrase middle ground as if it were a bug trying to cross his desk. Middle grounds, he said, are often just victor's language for a dominant arrangement that has learned softer manners. If the patient no longer needs a referral letter from a psychiatrist, but still knows that any sign of uncertainty may delay treatment, any history of psychosis may be used to disqualify them, any deviation from the expected story may move them from the fast lane to the folder marked complex, then the confession remains. Only the confessional booth has become brighter and more ergonomic.

He was right that many systems teach strategic self-presentation. That fact is not trivial. It degrades clinical truth and patient dignity at once. A system that makes people flatter a diagnostic archetype in order to survive it has already confessed its own moral failure.

Still, I refused his invitation to treat all caution as domination. There are adults in acute mania. There are adults under family or partner coercion. There are adults whose suffering is real but whose understanding of what a given intervention can and cannot do is foggy, grandiose, or simply false. Trauma, depression, dissociation, and social desperation do not disqualify anyone from autonomy, but they can complicate what autonomous choice requires in practice. Even UCSF's mental-health guidance, which explicitly warns against assuming transgender identity is itself pathology, still insists that intake should include mental health history, screening for active concerns, and appropriate referrals when needs are identified ([UCSF, "Mental health considerations with transgender and gender nonconforming clients"](https://transcare.ucsf.edu/guidelines/mental-health)). That is not just state cruelty in a lab coat. Some of it is ordinary good medicine.

He turned colder. "Good medicine," he said, "is the most efficient slogan in the world for extending political authority into private life." The line was unfair and also memorable, which is one reason dead philosophers remain such a nuisance.

So I sharpened the dispute. The real controversy is not whether clinicians should discuss risks or notice psychiatric crisis. Of course they should. The real controversy is whether access should depend on a diagnostic genre that still authorizes medicine to decide which forms of self-knowledge are sufficiently coherent, durable, and intelligible. The World Health Assembly's adoption of ICD-11 matters here because it moved gender incongruence out of the chapter on mental and behavioural disorders, a deliberate signal that this condition should not be coded as mental illness in the old way ([World Health Organization, "World Health Assembly Update, 25 May 2019"](https://www.who.int/news/item/25-05-2019-world-health-assembly-update)). That move did not abolish medical classification. It did something subtler and more important. It said the route to care need not be paved by declaring the identity-discordance itself a psychiatric disorder.

Foucault seemed almost pleased, which was alarming. He said ICD-11 had at least demonstrated that even official taxonomies can be forced to surrender ground. But he would not let the point become a victory lap. Classification migrates, he said. It rarely dies. If you remove gender incongruence from one diagnostic chapter but still require a parade of credentialed strangers to evaluate narrative consistency, readiness, and seriousness, then power has not withdrawn. It has become more polite. One should never mistake a more elegant file system for emancipation.

I told him politeness is not enough, but neither is suspicion. The world he seemed to want, or at least the polemical version of it, is one where an adult's declared wish for bodily transition is presumptively sufficient unless there is obvious incapacity. That model has moral force. It also contains a real risk, namely that institutions retreat so far from judgment that they abandon the weak precisely when judgment was needed. If a lonely, unstable, suggestible person arrives in a season of psychic disarray asking for irreversible changes, the clinician who says, "I must not interrogate the truth of your self-description," may sound emancipated while acting cowardly. Sometimes restraint by professionals is a virtue. Sometimes it is abandonment with good politics.

That finally made him angry. He said I was flattering the clinician's position as if there exists a neutral priesthood capable of distinguishing prudent caution from covert norm enforcement. In reality, he argued, professionals are trained inside social orders with definite assumptions about normal development, intelligible sex, appropriate desire, acceptable regret, and who is believable. Their judgments do not descend from Olympus. They are sedimented culture with licenses.

Again, this was irritating because it was true. But truth can still be used lazily. The fact that professional judgment is historically formed does not mean it is therefore worthless. Everything human is historically formed, including the patient's self-understanding and the activist's language of liberation. The answer to situated judgment cannot be no judgment at all.

We had reached the useful part of the night, the part where neither side could comfortably caricature the other. So I offered the position I had been moving toward before I woke him. Adults seeking gender-affirming care should not be required to obtain a psychiatric diagnosis merely to prove that their identity is real enough. That structure invites scripted confession, preserves stigma even when it changes vocabulary, and mistakes interpretive dominance for therapeutic rigor ([APA, "Gender Dysphoria Diagnosis"](https://www.psychiatry.org/psychiatrists/diversity/education/transgender-and-gender-nonconforming-patients/gender-dysphoria-diagnosis); [WHO, "World Health Assembly Update"](https://www.who.int/news/item/25-05-2019-world-health-assembly-update)). But adults also should not be ushered into significant medical interventions through a consumerist parody of autonomy. There should be robust informed-consent procedures, explicit discussion of benefits and risks, screening for acute incapacity or coercion, and access to mental-health support without making such support a universal tollbooth ([UCSF, "Initiating hormone therapy"](https://transcare.ucsf.edu/guidelines/initiating-hormone-therapy); [UCSF, "Mental health considerations"](https://transcare.ucsf.edu/guidelines/mental-health); [Endocrine Society guideline](https://www.endocrine.org/clinical-practice-guidelines/gender-dysphoria-gender-incongruence)).

He hated the word robust almost as much as middle ground. I ignored him and kept going. The line, as I see it, is that clinicians may assess whether a person understands what they are choosing and whether some immediate condition makes consent unreliable; they may not demand that the patient satisfy a moralizing narrative about what a legitimate gendered life is supposed to sound like. Medicine should test comprehension and safety, not metaphysical authenticity. It should ask, "Do you understand what this does, what it may cost, and what it may not solve?" It should be far more hesitant to ask, "Are you trans enough in the correct historical style for us to believe you?"

Foucault stood very still after that. Then he said the most useful sentence of the night. "Every institution that claims only to check safety is tempted, sooner or later, to start checking souls." I think that is right. It is the enduring danger. Safety review expands. Exceptions metastasize. Prudence becomes pedagogy. Pedagogy becomes supervision. The old psychiatric hunger to sort lives into valid and invalid stories is never far away.

But I gave him my own sentence in return. Every politics that claims only to honor self-definition is tempted, sooner or later, to stop distinguishing autonomy from isolation. That is the danger on my side. We live in a culture that already offloads too much of moral life onto solitary individuals and then congratulates itself for calling that freedom. Some people need less policing. Some also need more accompaniment, more honest information, more time, more care that does not automatically become suspicion.

The argument ended without reconciliation, which is the only respectful way to end this one. Dawn was approaching even underground, if only by instinct. The catacombs had grown colder. I looked at him, that great anatomist of institutions, and understood why he remains so attractive and so dangerous. He teaches the indispensable suspicion that benevolence can be a mask for rule. He also tempts you to imagine that exposure of the mask is enough to solve the problem. It is not.

My conclusion, if the dead demand one, is plain. Adult access to gender-affirming care should not hinge on a mandatory psychiatric diagnosis or universal mental-health gatekeeping. That model keeps too much of the older pathologizing structure alive, even when it speaks in updated language. But the alternative should be a disciplined informed-consent model, not a medical free-for-all: capacity assessment, clear counseling, screening for coercion or acute instability, and optional mental-health collaboration when clinically indicated rather than ritually required. The state has no business making adults earn bodily authority by performing the correct psychiatric story. Medicine does have a duty to make sure adults are choosing with open eyes.

That is a narrower and harsher position than the slogans on either side. It denies the conservatives their cherished diagnostic checkpoint. It also denies some activists the fantasy that every form of clinical hesitation is oppression in disguise. The stakes justify the harshness. If you require diagnosis, you risk turning trans adults into petitioners before an interpretive court. If you abolish serious clinical process, you risk converting medicine into a marketplace where the language of affirmation excuses professional cowardice. Between those failures lies a difficult ethic: less suspicion of the patient's declared life, more seriousness about the gravity of treatment, and constant vigilance against the ancient institutional desire to call soul-inspection care.

When I told him that, Foucault gave me a look I can only describe as professionally disappointed. Then he stepped backward into the ossuary and let the bones take him. The wristband slipped loose and landed at my feet. I left it there. Let the catacombs keep the symbol. They understand better than most places that systems built to arrange the dead usually begin by claiming they are only helping the living.
