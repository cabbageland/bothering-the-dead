today is April 25, 2026.

I found Jeremy Bentham where he had arranged to be found. That felt important. Most graves conceal, or at least attempt the decency of concealment. Bentham designed himself for exhibition. I went to University College London, into the building where his Auto-Icon now sits in a glass case, and there he was: preserved skeleton, his clothes, a wax head where the real one failed the aesthetic test, the whole stubborn apparatus of a man who tried to remain useful after death. UCL still tells the story in the plain institutional voice that somehow makes it stranger: Bentham directed in his will that his body be preserved, seated, dressed in his own black clothes, and brought out when friends of "the greatest happiness system of morals and legislation" gathered to commemorate him ([UCL Bentham Project](https://www.ucl.ac.uk/laws/research/research-projects/bentham-project/about-jeremy-bentham/auto-icon)).

It is one thing to know that in the abstract. It is another to stand in front of the case and feel that the argument has become furniture.

So I woke him there. Not with thunder, not with Latin, not with the theatrical nonsense resurrection usually attracts, but by refusing the usual museum etiquette. I put my palm to the glass and addressed him as if the glass were rude. I said: you wanted to go on being used by the living. Fine. Sit up and defend the whole rotten principle.

He opened his eyes with the impatience of a man annoyed to discover that posterity is less orderly than he had hoped. The wax face did not move, but the mind arrived all at once. He adjusted himself in the chair like a lecturer settling into a familiar grievance.

"You have come," he said, "to object to utility again."

Not utility exactly. I had come to object to the moral habit that so often travels under its flag: the conversion of the dead into resources. Bentham is the worst possible dead man to choose if one wants easy moral comfort, because he volunteered. He not only donated himself. He theorized the donation. He imagined the dead continuing to serve the living, and his own remains still make that claim in public space. If anyone deserved to answer for the ethics of display, retention, and pedagogical use of human remains, it was the man who had turned himself into a case study.

I told him the subject plainly. Our controversy would be this: whether museums, universities, and states are justified in retaining and displaying human remains for education, research, and public culture when the remains are useful, even if many of the dead never consented, and even if descendant communities demand return.

He brightened. Controversy suited him.

Bentham began where Bentham always begins. Nature, he reminded me, has placed mankind under the governance of pleasure and pain; the principle of utility judges actions by their tendency to augment happiness or diminish suffering ([Bentham, *An Introduction to the Principles of Morals and Legislation*, ch. I](https://www.utilitarianism.com/jeremy-bentham/index.html)). The dead, he said, no longer suffer physically. The living do. If a skeleton in a case can teach anatomy, history, law, empire, mortality, or the contingency of custom, then the question is not whether display feels eerie, but whether it produces more good than harm. Sentimental disgust is not an argument.

That first move was powerful because it was not stupid. There really are cases where the dead teach the living. Medical schools use cadavers. Forensic collections have helped identify the murdered. Museums can expose histories of violence that polite nations prefer to forget. The easy line, that all display of the dead is desecration, collapses as soon as we remember that knowledge is often purchased from mortality.

But that was not the real question, and I told him so. The real question was who gets to decide what counts as use, and who pays the moral price. Bentham had arranged his own body into a philosophical exhibit. Fine. His consent matters. It matters a great deal. But the museum world is not built out of Bentham cases. It is built, far too often, out of conquest, extraction, grave robbery, racial science, imperial classification, and institutions that learned to call possession curation. The National Park Service's summary of NAGPRA is blunt about what the law was for: Congress recognized in 1990 that Native American human remains "must at all times be treated with dignity and respect," and that remains and related cultural items taken from federal or tribal lands belong first to lineal descendants, tribes, and Native Hawaiian organizations ([NPS, NAGPRA overview](https://www.nps.gov/subjects/nagpra/index.htm)). The law itself defines human remains and funerary objects not as neutral specimens but as part of burial sites and cultural items bound to specific communities ([25 U.S.C. § 3001](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/25/3001)).

Bentham frowned, which on him looked like increased architecture. "You are shifting ground," he said. "If the complaint is theft, then condemn theft. If the complaint is violence, then condemn violence. But do not let the abuses of acquisition obscure the value of preservation and display once the materials exist. If the remains can now do good, why bury the good with the bones?"

Because the bones are not raw material, I said. Because what looks like neutral preservation from the museum side can be experienced, by descendants and by communities whose dead were taken, as an ongoing occupation. The point is not merely that the original seizure was wrongful, though often it was. The point is that continued institutional possession reproduces the same hierarchy of authority: the university keeps deciding that its curiosity outranks someone else's obligation to their dead.

He countered that obligations to the dead are themselves for the living. Ritual, reverence, taboo, mourning, ancestral continuity, piety, all of it cashes out in present human welfare. That did not make them trivial. It made them measurable. If public display of remains humiliates a community, fractures trust, or extends historical injury, utility can count that. But if careful curation, contextualization, and scholarship serve a wider good, then refusing display simply because the object is a body risks enthroning superstition over benefit.

This is where I stopped being generally critical and became specific. Institutions have already had to admit that "careful curation" is not enough. The United Kingdom's own *Guidance for the Care of Human Remains in Museums* was issued precisely because museums holding human remains required not just storage protocols but an ethical and legal framework, including a framework for claims of return; it explicitly notes the authority created by section 47 of the Human Tissue Act 2004 to de-accession certain remains under one thousand years old ([UK government guidance](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/guidance-for-the-care-of-human-remains-in-museums)). The International Council of Museums likewise treats ethics, restitution, legislation, and provenance as central museum obligations, not decorative extras ([ICOM Code of Ethics overview](https://icom.museum/en/resources/standards-guidelines/code-of-ethics/)). In other words, even the institutions most invested in keeping things have had to concede that possession is morally unstable.

Bentham pounced on the phrase. "Morally unstable is not morally forbidden."

True, I said. But instability matters when the object under dispute is a human being. A museum can own a pot in a way it cannot quite own a skull. Even law strains at the difference. We write special statutes. We draft special guidance. We invent the language of dignity because ordinary property talk is too crude for the case. The dead are the place where liberal ownership starts to show its seams.

He smiled at that, because he liked seams. Then he made his strongest argument. Suppose, he said, that a set of remains can illuminate a genocide, prove a massacre, expose racist pseudoscience, or teach ordinary visitors that empire once treated persons as collectible matter. Suppose display, done honestly, shocks the public out of moral laziness. Suppose concealment flatters the living by letting them say they have been respectful when in fact they have merely hidden the evidence. Would I still prefer repatriation, burial, silence?

That was the hardest moment because he had finally reached the point where the dead really do accuse the living by remaining visible. There are times when the corpse is testimony. There are times when burial can serve amnesia better than dignity. And Bentham, infuriatingly, understood that moral language often becomes a laundering mechanism for discomfort. Sometimes people call a display disrespectful because they do not want to look at what their civilization did.

So I gave him the answer he deserved instead of the easy one. No, I do not think every display of human remains is wrong. I think the right default is not display but justification. The burden should fall heavily on the institution that wants to keep and show the dead. It should have to argue, publicly and concretely, that this particular retention serves a serious purpose that cannot be achieved by photographs, replicas, testimony, or consent-based alternatives; that provenance has been investigated; that descendant communities have been consulted where they exist; that the display does not merely convert domination into pedagogy and call the conversion enlightenment.

Bentham said that sounded suspiciously like rule-utilitarianism in mourning clothes.

Maybe it is. I am not allergic to consequences. I am allergic to institutions pretending that consequences are always theirs to total up. Bentham's error, or at least his temptation, is the temptation of every planner: to imagine that once values can be compared, someone competent will eventually be entitled to do the comparing. But death scrambles that confidence. The dead body is not just a datum. It is the point where knowledge, grief, kinship, religion, law, history, and power all touch the same object and call it by incompatible names.

He answered, quieter now, that incompatible names do not absolve us from deciding. The museum must decide. The state must decide. The university must decide. Even repatriation is a decision by the living about the dead. There is no escaping administration.

That, unfortunately, was also true. The fantasy that one can leave the dead alone is only a fantasy for the lucky dead, the ones already protected by family, custom, and social recognition. Many of the rest have already been processed into archives, drawers, laboratories, and exhibits. Someone must decide what justice looks like after the first injustice has already happened.

We had reached the place where real arguments stop feeling clean. Bentham wanted a world in which bodies could continue to serve reason after life. I wanted a world in which institutions could not too quickly rename appropriation as service. He trusted explicit public benefit more than inherited taboo. I trusted the moral warning contained in the stubborn fact that human remains keep producing special law, special language, and special shame.

When I finally let him go, it was with less certainty than I had expected and more hostility to simplification. Bentham settled back into his chair, not defeated, which would have been cheap, but clarified. His own case remains exceptional because it is saturated with consent. That is exactly why it is useful. It lets us see the difference between a dead person made public by his own philosophical will and the innumerable dead made public by someone else's appetite, empire, curiosity, or institutional convenience.

The stakes are not antiquarian. They are about what kind of civilization we become when knowledge and custody conflict, when descendants ask for return, when universities want to teach with bones, when museums discover that the line between preservation and possession is thinner than they hoped. Bentham wanted the dead to be useful to the living. Fair enough. But usefulness is not innocence. The question is never only whether the dead can still serve us. It is whether our way of using them teaches us to become less arrogant in the presence of persons, or merely more articulate about our right to keep what we have already taken.
