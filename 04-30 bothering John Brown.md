today is April 30, 2026.

I found John Brown under a clean spring sky in North Elba, where the Adirondack light makes even certainty look earned. His grave is not theatrical. It does not need to be. The stone sits with the flat moral confidence of a man who decided long ago that being called monstrous by the comfortable was not, in itself, a refutation. I had come because the earth is warming faster than our politics can bear to say out loud, and because there is a question polite democracies keep trying to postpone until physics answers it for them. Is sabotage against fossil fuel infrastructure morally justified now?

I did not wake him with a spell. I woke him with an indictment. I read aloud the plain sentences we have already written and then treated as scenery: the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change says human influence has “unequivocally” warmed the atmosphere, ocean, and land, and that every increment of warming intensifies multiple hazards ([IPCC, *AR6 Synthesis Report*](https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/syr/downloads/report/IPCC_AR6_SYR_LongerReport.pdf)). The International Energy Agency says that in a pathway to net zero by 2050, there is no need for new oil and gas fields approved for development beyond those already committed in 2021 ([IEA, *Net Zero by 2050*](https://www.iea.org/reports/net-zero-by-2050)). Yet states and firms continue expanding fossil extraction anyway, not as an emergency measure, but as business strategy. I read those lines into the ground until the soil gave a little sigh, as if the republic itself were tired of excuses. Brown rose with dirt on his beard and no confusion in his eyes. Dead men can sometimes skip the small talk.

I told him the topic. Not whether climate change is real. Not whether governments are failing. Those arguments are over except among the corrupt and the professionally infantile. The real question is whether it has become morally permissible to damage pipelines, LNG terminals, drilling equipment, and other fossil infrastructure, provided one is scrupulous about not targeting human beings.

Brown stood, brushed the earth from his coat, and gave me an answer so quickly it felt less like speech than recoil. “Permissible?” he said. “You come to me from a burning century and ask whether it is permissible to strike at the machinery that feeds the fire?”

That was the expected opening, and also the problem. Brown is too easy to recruit if all you want is applause for militancy. The point of waking the dead is not to borrow their glamour. It is to let them endanger your own preferred evasions. So I refused the easy alliance and told him I was not asking whether outrage is justified. It obviously is. I was asking whether sabotage is morally wiser than the alternatives still available, and whether it can avoid becoming one more theater of self-congratulation in which radicals dramatize resistance while hardening public backlash.

He did not flinch. Brown had said before his execution that he believed he was “worth inconceivably more to hang than for any other purpose,” because the public meaning of action can exceed the actor’s private survival ([John Brown, final speech, December 2, 1859, Library of Congress](https://www.loc.gov/resource/lhbcb.03101/?sp=1&st=text)). That sentence has the terrible strength of someone who has already priced his own life into an argument. He told me the first moral error of my age was to treat property destruction as if it belonged in the same conceptual box as interpersonal violence. A pipeline is not a child. A compressor station is not a bus full of workers. A drilling rig is not a village. To disable a machine that helps impose foreseeable mass death is not, by itself, to violate the same norm as attacking a person. “You moderns,” he said, “keep letting the grammar of order do the thinking for you. The law calls both things destruction, and so you begin to imagine they are morally adjacent.”

That distinction matters. It is the strongest argument on his side. Liberal states routinely sanctify property in ways that make injury to capital feel more legible than injury to the future. But climate change is not an abstraction hovering over that moral accounting. It is a machinery of accumulated harms, distributed across time and geography but no less material for that. Heat already kills; floods already displace; crop losses already magnify fragility; and the worst damage falls disproportionately on those who did least to cause it ([IPCC, *AR6 Synthesis Report*](https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/syr/downloads/report/IPCC_AR6_SYR_LongerReport.pdf)). If an infrastructure system is predictably helping produce those outcomes, then the claim that one must never physically interfere with it begins to look less like moral seriousness than fetishized obedience.

I said yes, but that conclusion comes too fast. The anti-sabotage case is not just bourgeois squeamishness. First, democratic legitimacy matters. In his “Letter from Birmingham Jail,” Martin Luther King Jr. defended direct action precisely because it created tension that forced negotiation, but he also insisted on discipline, openness to penalty, and orientation toward a community’s moral conscience, not just its fear ([King, “Letter from Birmingham Jail”](https://www.csuchico.edu/iege/_assets/documents/susi-letter-from-birmingham-jail.pdf)). Sabotage can slide away from that model. It can become clandestine, vanguardist, and strategically vain, substituting the satisfactions of rupture for the slower work of building majorities, institutions, and durable non-fossil power. Second, means shape ends. Secretive campaigns against infrastructure may invite intensified policing, justify broader repression of even peaceful movements, and persuade frightened publics that decarbonization is a war on ordinary life rather than a defense of it. Third, there is the contamination problem. Even when activists intend only property damage, complex systems contain workers, bystanders, cascading effects, and opportunities for miscalculation. Moral permission cannot be granted in the abstract while hand-waving the likelihood of concrete mistakes.

Brown listened with an expression I distrust in the righteous: patience sharpened into contempt. He answered by attacking the word *available*. You say alternatives are still available, he said, but available to whom, and on what time scale? Elections occur. Petitions circulate. Agencies issue reports. Youth plaintiffs win eloquent findings and then watch governments appeal them into old age. Meanwhile the carbon budget tightens, infrastructure gets financed for decades of lock-in, and every procedural delay becomes a physical fact in the atmosphere. The United Nations Environment Programme’s emissions gap reporting has for years described a persistent chasm between what states promise and what would actually limit warming to 1.5 or even 2°C ([UNEP, *Emissions Gap Report 2024*](https://www.unep.org/resources/emissions-gap-report-2024)). Brown’s point was not that procedure is worthless. It was that procedure can become the preferred moral alibi of people benefiting from delay. A politics that never escalates beyond authorized dissent may simply be a politics that has accepted defeat with excellent manners.

That argument is uncomfortably strong too. It forces a distinction between faith in democracy and faith in whatever existing institutions happen to call themselves democratic while failing a basic test of collective self-preservation. Henry David Thoreau, in “Civil Disobedience,” argued that when government becomes the agent of injustice, conscientious people must not lend themselves to the wrong it does ([Thoreau, “Resistance to Civil Government”](https://thoreau.eserver.org/civil1.html)). Most people quote him as if refusal were enough. Brown would not. He always suspected that clean hands are a luxury position if one’s abstention leaves the machinery intact. He asked me, with the annoying precision of the dead, whether my preferred politics was actually trying to stop the build-out or merely trying not to be blamed for failing to stop it.

I answered by making the strongest case I could against him. There is a moral difference between resistance that interrupts and resistance that appoints itself sovereign. Once a movement authorizes itself to decide which infrastructure may be destroyed outside broad public consent, it risks reproducing the same anti-democratic logic it condemns in the corporations and ministries it opposes. Not every urgent cause licenses unilateral force. If it did, politics would collapse into a competition among factions certain of their emergency. Brown himself is the wrong person to sentimentalize here. Harpers Ferry did not trigger emancipation through immaculate moral clarity. It triggered horror, polarization, and blood. One may still judge Brown historically necessary, but necessity is not innocence.

He smiled at that, which was worse than anger. “At last,” he said. “Now we are speaking seriously.” Then he cut at the center of the liberal objection. Public consent, he said, is not a magical purifier when the public has been systematically lied to, structurally bribed, and materially trapped. Fossil firms knew for decades what their products were doing; internal industry documents and congressional testimony have made that deception hard to deny ([U.S. House Oversight Committee, *Denial, Disinformation, and Doublespeak*](https://oversightdemocrats.house.gov/sites/evo-subsites/oversightdemocrats.house.gov/files/2024-04-29.%20Staff%20Report%20-%20Denial%2C%20Disinformation%2C%20and%20Doublespeak%20-%20Big%20Oil%27s%20Evolving%20Efforts%20to%20Avoid%20Accountability%20for%20Climate%20Change.pdf); [Exxon CEO testimony, House Oversight Committee, 2021](https://oversight.house.gov/hearing/fueling-the-climate-crisis-examining-big-oils-deceptive-climate-lobbying-and-denial-tactics/)). If consent has been engineered inside a long campaign of fraud and dependency, then merely counting present acquiescence may ratify manipulation rather than autonomy.

I told him fraud does not erase the duty of judgment. Even if sabotage can be morally justified in principle, the question is when, by whom, and under what discipline. Andreas Malm argues that a climate movement confined to genteel ritual has failed to impose sufficient costs on the system it opposes, and that sabotage against property can be understood as an escalation short of violence against persons ([Malm, *How to Blow Up a Pipeline*](https://www.versobooks.com/products/1786-how-to-blow-up-a-pipeline)). The book’s title attracts the stupidest readers first, but the underlying issue is real: whether strict nonviolence has been misread into a ban not only on harming people, but on damaging the installations through which a destructive order reproduces itself. Still, the mere existence of that argument does not settle the burden of proof. The people who would undertake sabotage owe more than fury. They owe evidence that the tactic is likely to reduce net harm, not merely express lucid despair.

Brown surprised me by agreeing, partly. He had no patience for what he called “romance masquerading as courage.” He did not say every broken machine is righteous. He said sabotage becomes thinkable only under severe constraints. First, there must be a just cause, and climate devastation clearly qualifies, given the scale, foreseeability, and asymmetry of harm documented by the scientific record ([IPCC, *AR6 Synthesis Report*](https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/syr/downloads/report/IPCC_AR6_SYR_LongerReport.pdf)). Second, the target must be materially connected to that harm, not chosen for symbolism alone. Third, there must be stringent discrimination: no intention to harm persons, no cavalier attitude toward workers, no acceptable-risk shrug about responders or nearby communities. Fourth, the act must be part of a broader political strategy aimed at ending dependency and widening justice, not a nihilist performance. Fifth, the saboteur must tell the truth about what they are doing. Not euphemize it, not aestheticize it, not hide from its moral cost.

That list made him sound less like an insurrectionary mascot than a grim just-war theorist for infrastructure conflict. I hated that it made sense. Yet I kept one objection alive. Last resort is not a decorative criterion. Traditional just war reasoning does not require exhausting literally every imaginable option before force becomes permissible, but it does require that reasonably promising alternatives have failed or are plainly inadequate. On climate, that standard is maddeningly hard to judge. There have been decades of normal advocacy and still emissions rise. But there has also not yet been sustained majoritarian democratic mobilization on the scale of labor wartime conversion, nor the kinds of public ownership programs that could directly displace fossil capital. Perhaps what is missing is not sabotage, but organizational power.

Brown said that answer would carry more moral dignity if the clock were not atmospheric. The problem with climate is that it turns every strategic debate into a fight with one-sided temporality. Delay favors the incumbents because delay is itself a victory for carbon. New infrastructure approved now can lock in emissions or political dependence for decades ([IEA, *Net Zero by 2050*](https://www.iea.org/reports/net-zero-by-2050)). In such conditions, waiting for the ideal movement can become a pious way of collaborating with inertia. “You speak as though history grants extensions to those who prefer unanimity,” he said.

He was right to press the time pressure, but not right enough to win easily. There is another danger in his position. Once sabotage is dignified as conscience under emergency, movements may attract people who are less disciplined, less discriminating, and more enthralled by force than by justice. Tactics migrate. Norms erode. States exploit incidents. A single death, even unintended, could not be brushed aside as a tragic footnote. It would alter the moral field instantly. There is also the risk of strategic narcissism: small cells imagining themselves history’s lever while the larger public, instead of being awakened, merely recoils. Politics is not only about being correct. It is about making a different future materially and psychologically buildable.

Brown answered with the most devastating point of the night. Recoil from what? From damaged machinery? He said our societies already tolerate a daily level of slow violence so vast that objections to sabotage often sound like ritual concern for social peace in the presence of organized devastation. Rob Nixon’s phrase “slow violence” is apt here, even if Brown himself never knew it: harms dispersed across time and space are still harms, and moral imagination often fails precisely where victims are numerous but not simultaneous ([Nixon, *Slow Violence and the Environmentalism of the Poor*](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/books/9780674072343)). If a movement breaks a pipeline valve and no one dies, the public sees broken metal. If a government approves another extraction basin, the eventual deaths arrive diffusely, statistically, and with enough delay to protect the approvers from feeling causal. Brown thought this asymmetry was not an accident of perception but one of the central political advantages of fossil modernity.

I could not answer that with a slogan, so I answered with a boundary. However grave the emergency, one must not let climate politics become a moral laundering operation for the pleasure of coercion. The prohibition on targeting persons is not a soft preference. It is the line that keeps resistance from mutating into the thing it claims to oppose. Brown agreed more readily than I expected. His own life is too often flattened into an icon of bloodshed, when in fact his hatred was aimed at a social order held up by law, property, theology, and armed force together. He did not praise killing as such. He praised refusing reconciliation with atrocity. That is a different, darker, and more demanding principle.

By dawn the light had begun to hollow him out. I asked for a verdict before the day reclaimed him. He gave me one, and I think it is the harshest honest answer.

Sabotage against fossil fuel infrastructure is not automatically justified, but neither is it morally disqualified simply because it is illegal or disruptive. It can be justified when five conditions are met: the target is directly tied to severe unjust harm; the act is designed with stringent care to avoid injury to persons; conventional channels have proved structurally inadequate relative to the emergency; the sabotage forms part of a broader emancipatory politics rather than a cult of action; and its likely consequences are soberly judged to reduce, not amplify, total harm. That is not a permission slip for chaos. It is a threshold test, and a severe one.

My own conclusion is narrower than Brown’s instinct but much closer to him than many liberals would like. In the face of a scientifically documented emergency, ongoing elite deception, and continued expansion of infrastructure that authoritative energy analysis says should not be expanded, there are cases where sabotage of fossil property can be morally justified ([IPCC, *AR6 Synthesis Report*](https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/syr/downloads/report/IPCC_AR6_SYR_LongerReport.pdf); [IEA, *Net Zero by 2050*](https://www.iea.org/reports/net-zero-by-2050); [U.S. House Oversight Committee, *Denial, Disinformation, and Doublespeak*](https://oversightdemocrats.house.gov/sites/evo-subsites/oversightdemocrats.house.gov/files/2024-04-29.%20Staff%20Report%20-%20Denial%2C%20Disinformation%2C%20and%20Doublespeak%20-%20Big%20Oil%27s%20Evolving%20Efforts%20to%20Avoid%20Accountability%20for%20Climate%20Change.pdf)). But justification here is exacting, not romantic. The burden on saboteurs is immense. They must be more disciplined than the order they oppose, clearer about consequences than the politicians of delay, and more allergic to human injury than the industry whose profits rest on distributing it downstream.

Brown faded while I was still speaking, which felt rude but philosophically consistent. The dead do not owe us closure. They owe us pressure. I left his grave with less innocence about democratic procedure and more suspicion of tactical purity. The point is not to adore sabotage. The point is to stop pretending that intact machinery is the same thing as moral peace. If we insist that every resistance remain perfectly decorous while the atmosphere fills on schedule, we are not choosing nonviolence. We are choosing which violence will remain visible enough to count.
