today is May 6, 2026.

I found Martin Luther King Jr. under a tasteful rain in Atlanta, beneath the polished stone that has been asked to perform serenity for a nation that still cannot decide whether it loves him or merely loves being seen loving him. The water around the crypt was dark and calm. Tourists had left the place hours ago. The gift shop was closed. The bronze inscriptions kept their official voice. Nearby, the country went on building data centers, pipelines, detention camps, luxury towers in floodplains, and ever more efficient ways to call catastrophe development.

I had brought no talisman except impatience. I spoke his name aloud, then read from the old documents that still embarrass the living: the warning in the “Letter from Birmingham Jail” that “justice too long delayed is justice denied” and that the real obstacle is not the open segregationist but the white moderate “more devoted to ‘order’ than to justice” ([King, 1963](https://www.africa.upenn.edu/Articles_Gen/Letter_Birmingham.html)). I read from *Where Do We Go from Here* that “riots are the language of the unheard,” not because he admired riots, but because he refused the moral laziness of condemning the fire while protecting the conditions that strike the match ([King, 1967](https://www.crmvet.org/docs/otheram.htm#where)). Then I added a newer scripture, one the dead had not heard: the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change concluding with high confidence that climate change is already causing widespread losses, damages, and in some cases irreversible impacts to human and natural systems ([IPCC AR6 Synthesis Report, 2023](https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/syr/)).

That did it. Not magic exactly. More like jurisdiction. The slab took a breath. Water trembled. King rose with the exhausted dignity of a man summoned back into an argument he suspected had never really paused.

He looked at the reflecting pool, then at the city lights. “You have not called me for comfort,” he said.

“No,” I told him. “I called you because the world is heating past the language of reform, and I want to know whether sabotaging fossil fuel infrastructure can be morally justified.”

He gave me a tired, almost amused look, the look of someone recognizing a species of trouble before hearing its details. “You have come to ask a Baptist preacher,” he said, “whether destruction can be an instrument of justice.”

“A specific kind of destruction,” I said. “Not terror against persons. Not murder. Targeted disabling of pipelines, private jets, drilling equipment, server farms built to intensify extraction, perhaps the machinery of a lethal political economy. The International Energy Agency said in 2021 that reaching net-zero emissions globally requires no new oil and gas fields approved for development beyond projects already committed ([IEA, *Net Zero by 2050*](https://www.iea.org/reports/net-zero-by-2050)). But governments and firms continue approving expansion. If legal channels are structurally captured, when does sabotage become the lesser violence?”

He did not answer immediately. He touched the edge of the tomb as if reacquainting himself with the geometry of the living world. “You have framed the question in the vocabulary of emergency,” he said. “That vocabulary is often the prelude to license.”

“It is also sometimes the prelude to honesty.”

We began walking. In my imagination, or perhaps in his resurrection's temporary logic, the path around the crypt lengthened into a night garden of red clay and soaked magnolia leaves. The city receded enough to let argument become audible.

King said the first thing plainly: “I knew the temptation to sanctify coercion. But my conviction was not only that nonviolence was tactically superior. It was that violence and degradation corrupt the agent as well as the target. In *Pilgrimage to Nonviolence*, I wrote that nonviolence does not seek to defeat or humiliate the opponent, but to win friendship and understanding ([King, 1960](https://kinginstitute.stanford.edu/king-papers/documents/pilgrimage-nonviolence)). If you train a movement to treat breaking as its native language, do not be surprised when people become easier to break than machines.”

This is the strongest thing ever said against political romanticism: not that force fails, but that it recruits the soul into its methods. I felt its force immediately. Still, the earth was warm under our feet in a way that made piety seem like procrastination.

“So let us distinguish,” I said. “There is violence against persons, and there is force against property. Law often collapses them because property has arranged to be sacred. Morality should not. A slave who damages the wagon carrying him south is not the moral equal of the kidnapper. Workers who disable a machine chewing through their lungs are not mirror images of the owner. And if a pipeline will predictably intensify floods, crop failures, deadly heat, displacement, and mass death, then the pipeline is not innocent merely because it is inanimate. It is organized harm in steel form.”

King stopped. “That is an argument from complicity,” he said.

“Yes. And from urgency. The climate system does not grade our intentions kindly. The IPCC has warned that some adaptation limits are already reached and that each increment of warming increases losses and damages ([IPCC, 2023](https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/syr/)). The International Court of Justice has now stated that states have obligations under international law to protect the climate system from anthropogenic greenhouse-gas emissions, and that failure can constitute an internationally wrongful act ([ICJ Advisory Opinion summary, 2025](https://www.icj-cij.org/case/187)). If governments know this and still permit expansion, then legality itself is participating in crime. Why should citizens owe deference to a process that is openly arranging unlivable futures?”

He answered with what sounded at first like sorrow, then resolved into discipline. “Because the question is not whether the process is innocent. It is whether the method you propose can generate a more just order rather than merely dramatize desperation. In Birmingham we broke unjust laws openly and accepted punishment to reveal the brutality hidden inside procedural respectability ([King, 1963](https://www.africa.upenn.edu/Articles_Gen/Letter_Birmingham.html)). Sabotage, by contrast, often depends on concealment. It can inspire, yes. It can also detach the actor from the public they claim to save. A politics of exemplary damage may flatter the conscience of the few while leaving the many unconvinced, frightened, or invited into repression.”

I told him that this objection lands hardest when sabotage is theatrical but strategically thin. But that is not the only imaginable form. A campaign can be disciplined, selective, publicly reasoned, and paired with mass organizing, labor action, mutual aid, electoral pressure, treaty demands, and open civil disobedience. One need not worship the wrench. One can treat it as one instrument among others, justified when it prevents larger harms and constrained by rules stricter than the state's own. The comparison class is not utopia. It is the current order, which burns villages and calls the process permitting.

He looked at me sharply at that, because he knew the seduction of comparing oneself to empire and thereby excusing every excess. So I tightened the claim.

“I am not saying all destruction in the name of climate is justified. Most of it would be stupid. Some would be evil. I am saying there may be cases where disabling equipment that materially sustains catastrophic emissions is closer to defense than aggression. The tradition of civil disobedience already accepts that citizens may refuse cooperation with grave injustice. Thoreau argued that when the state imprisons unjustly, ‘the true place for a just man is also a prison’ ([Thoreau, *Civil Disobedience*](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/henry-david-thoreau-civil-disobedience)). But what if prison is too slow a witness for a fast-moving disaster? What if fidelity to life requires not only refusal, but interruption?”

At this he surprised me by not retreating to abstraction. He asked instead, “Would you disable a hospital generator powered by fossil fuel?”

“No.”

“A municipal bus depot?”

“Probably not, unless alternatives were secured. The point is not purification through chaos. The point is to target elite luxuries, new extraction, and infrastructure whose principal function is to deepen dependence while alternatives are blocked.”

“A private jet?”

“Yes.”

He almost smiled. “There at least your anger has found an object proportionate to its vanity.”

Then he turned severe again. “But every movement is judged not only by its targets, but by the anthropology implied by its methods. Nonviolence, as I understood it, wagers that people are not fixed in their moral roles. It leaves room for conversion. It is not sentimental. It is strategic faith about human plasticity. If you make sabotage central, you may tacitly declare that persuasion has ended and that enemies can only be impeded, not transformed. That is a grave declaration for a democratic movement to make.”

I think this was the deepest challenge he posed. Not, “Will property be damaged?” but, “What kind of creature do your methods assume the opponent to be?” A politics that grants no possibility of conversion can become spiritually efficient and politically barren.

But I could not concede the point whole. “There are institutions,” I said, “whose business model depends on treating every warning as a rounding error. Major fossil-fuel firms understood the climate threat decades ago, while funding doubt and delay ([Supran & Oreskes, 2017, summarizing ExxonMobil internal and public communications](https://doi.org/10.1088/1748-9326/aa815f)). Conversion may happen at the level of individuals. Meanwhile, the machine keeps signing leases. To insist on endlessly reoffering moral suasion to structures engineered for nonresponse can become its own form of fantasy. Sometimes you do not convert the bulldozer operator in time. You take the key.”

The rain had stopped. The whole place smelled briefly like wet stone and leaves. King folded his hands behind his back. “Then perhaps the truth,” he said, “is this. A movement against planetary ruin must be militant in discipline, radical in diagnosis, sacrificial in courage, and ruthless only against illusions. If it touches force against property, it must do so under conditions severe enough to prevent intoxication: absolute renunciation of injury to persons, public moral accounting, strategic subordination to mass democratic aims, and constant suspicion toward the ego's desire to feel heroic. Otherwise the means will educate the movement into something it did not intend to become.”

That was not permission. It was not exactly refusal either. It was a harder sentence than either. He had given sabotage no halo. He had also denied the comfort of pretending that order, as presently constituted, is peace.

We had come back to the tomb without my noticing. The reflecting pool held the first gray hint of morning. I understood, then, what the stakes of our argument actually were. Not whether a valve or a runway light or a drilling rig might ever be morally damaged, but whether democratic peoples can recover enough seriousness to distinguish life-serving force from domination, enough courage to interrupt systems killing them, and enough moral restraint to avoid becoming intimate with the very logic they oppose.

King placed his palm against the stone as if checking whether it still belonged to him. “The old question remains,” he said. “How shall we confront a massive structure of violence without reproducing its interior law?”

He stepped backward into the crypt, into the nation's unfinished conscience. Before the slab closed, he added one last line, almost gently: “Do not confuse moderation with morality. But do not confuse urgency with wisdom either.”

Then he was gone, and I was left with the obscene calm of the memorial, the city beginning again around it, and the unbearable fact that every serious climate argument now arrives trailing bodies from the future. Some of those bodies will be victims of storms, heat, hunger, border regimes, and wars intensified by a hotter world. Some will be victims of states suppressing disorder in the name of stability. A politics adequate to this century will have to answer for both.

That is why the question of sabotage will not go away. It should not be answered by adolescent thrill, nor by the property owner's superstition that damage to things is the ultimate sin. It must be answered at the level King demanded: by asking what kind of world the method makes, what kind of people it forms, what scale of suffering it prevents, and what residues of domination it leaves behind. The dead do not return to flatter us. They return, when bothered correctly, to make evasion more difficult.
